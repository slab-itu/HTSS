title  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A hierarchical model for estimating the spatial distribution and abundance of animals detected by continuous-time recorders  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Abstract  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Motivation  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Several spatial capture-recapture (SCR) models have been developed to estimate animal abundance by analyzing the detections of individuals in a spatial array of traps.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most of these models do not use the actual dates and times of detection, even though this information is readily available when using continuous-time recorders, such as microphones or motion-activated cameras.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Instead most SCR models either partition the period of trap operation into a set of subjectively chosen discrete intervals and ignore multiple detections of the same individual within each interval, or they simply use the frequency of detections during the period of trap operation and ignore the observed times of detection.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Both practices make inefficient use of potentially important information in the data.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Model_and_data_analysis  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We developed a hierarchical SCR model to estimate the spatial distribution and abundance of animals detected with continuous-time recorders.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our model includes two kinds of point processes: a spatial process to specify the distribution of latent activity centers of individuals within the region of sampling and a temporal process to specify temporal patterns in the detections of individuals.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We illustrated this SCR model by analyzing spatial and temporal patterns evident in the camera-trap detections of tigers living in and around the Nagarahole Tiger Reserve in India.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We also conducted a simulation study to examine the performance of our model when analyzing data sets of greater complexity than the tiger data.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Benefits  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Our approach provides three important benefits: First, it exploits all of the information in SCR data obtained using continuous-time recorders.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Second, it is sufficiently versatile to allow the effects of both space use and behavior of animals to be specified as functions of covariates that vary over space and time.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Third, it allows both the spatial distribution and abundance of individuals to be estimated, effectively providing a species distribution model, even in cases where spatial covariates of abundance are unknown or unavailable.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We illustrated these benefits in the analysis of our data, which allowed us to quantify differences between nocturnal and diurnal activities of tigers and to estimate their spatial distribution and abundance across the study area.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our continuous-time SCR model allows an analyst to specify many of the ecological processes thought to be involved in the distribution, movement, and behavior of animals detected in a spatial trapping array of continuous-time recorders.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We plan to extend this model to estimate the population dynamics of animals detected during multiple years of SCR surveys.  #@NEW_LINE#@#

Introduction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Spatial capture-recapture (SCR) models provide a statistical framework for estimating the density of individuals in a population, that is, the number of individuals per unit area in a finite region of two-dimensional, Euclidean space [1, 2].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In contrast to non-spatial models, SCR models use the detection histories and the locations of marked individuals.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  An important component of SCR models is the spatial point process that specifies the location of each individuals center of activity (or home range in some cases).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The activity center of an individual is not directly observable, but it is assumed to be fixed during the period of sampling.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This latter assumption can be regarded as a form of demographic closure for SCR models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Some of the earliest SCR models were developed for the analysis of detections of naturally marked animals at a set of fixed locations [3, 4].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Natural markings allow individuals to be identified uniquely and come in a variety of forms owing to technological advances in non-invasive sampling methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, body markings (stripe or spot patterns) can be used to distinguish individual tigers or leopards, and acoustic profiles can be used to distinguish the vocalizations of individual songbirds or whales (at least for some species).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Alternatively, DNA obtained from scats or hair snares can be used to identify individuals.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Different types of natural markings obviously require different types of detectors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Here we focus on SCR surveys that satisfy two design requirements: (1) individuals can potentially be observed at more than one of several fixed locations during the period of sampling and (2) the date and time of each observation is recorded, as is common when using microphones or motion-activated cameras as detectors [5, 6].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To satisfy the first requirement, detectors should be placed at locations based on the likely range of an individuals movements.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These placements induce heterogeneity in the rates of detection among individuals because animals whose activity centers lie within the interior of a spatial array of detectors are more likely to be observed than animals whose activity centers lie near the edge of the array.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Although several SCR models have been developed with the first design requirement in mind, most these models have ignored the continuous period of operation of the detectors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In some models the total period of sampling is partitioned into discrete intervals and a binary observation of 0 (no detections of an individual) or 1 (one or more detections of an individual) is used for each interval ([4] and [2], chapter 5).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This discretizaton of time is subjective and potentially uses data inefficiently because the same individual can be detected multiple times during a single interval.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Discretization into 24-hour intervals does not solve the problem because two detections of the same individual may be counted either once or twice depending on the choice of cut-off time (the so-called midnight problem [7]).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Other SCR models use the total number of detections of an individual by treating the total period of sampling as a single interval ([3] and [2], chapter 9); however, these models do not use information associated with the exact dates and times of detection.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To our knowledge, only one SCR model has been formulated for times of detection [6], and this model was developed primarily for the estimation of population density.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Here, we propose a hierarchical Bayesian model which includes many of the assumptions made by [6] but also allows inferences to be computed for the spatial distribution of individual activity centers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our approach may be used to construct species distribution models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It is useful even when the sources of spatial variation in population density are either unobserved or unknown.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We begin by describing the hierarchical model, which includes a spatial point process for the distribution of individual activity centers and a temporal point process for the distribution of detection times of each individual.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We show that this model is a logical extension of count-based SCR models that do not account for temporal heterogenity in detection rates.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We illustrate the benefits of our approach by analyzing the detection times of individual tigers observed in a spatial array of camera traps.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Materials_and_methods  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Model_description  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Assume a population of N individually identifiable animals living within a finite region B of Euclidean space (specifically, ) is surveyed using K camera traps whose locations are selected to be representative of B.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The trap locations need not be random but their spacing should permit any animal to be detected by more than one camera as a consequence of its movements.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We assume that each animal living within B moves around a fixed, center of activity, which we denote using s. Our goal is to infer the spatial distribution and abundance of the activity centers of all animals living within B using only the detection locations and times of animals observed during the camera-trap survey.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Camera-trap_survey_of_tigers  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We surveyed the population of tigers (Panthera tigris) living within and adjacent to the Nagarahole Tiger Reserve of Karnataka, India using a spatial array of 162 motion-activated camera traps (Fig 2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Ecological descriptions of the study area and species were provided by [16, 17] and [18].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Each camera trap included two inward facing cameras so that both flanks of an individual tiger could be photographed [19].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Camera trap locations were selected to increase detections using prior knowledge of the behavior and range of movements of individual tigers [4, 17].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Each camera trap operated continuously during the 45-day period of sampling (26 Nov 2014 to 13 Jan 2015), recording the date and time when a tiger was photographed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Individual tigers were identified from photographs using their unique stripe patterns [16], as shown in Fig 3.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All field work was carried out under research permits issued by the Karnataka Forest Department and the Kerala Forest Department, in forest areas administered by these agencies.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  No other approvals or permits were required because our non-invasive, camera-trap surveys did not involve any handling, collection, sampling or sacrifice of animals, or experimental manipulation of any type.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Camera traps were placed 23 km apart throughout the 860 km2 area to provide adequate exposure to all tigers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The spatial domain used to fit our SCR models also included a 10-km wide buffer where suitable habitat existed, thus yielding a total area of 1130 km2 (Fig 2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The width of this buffer was based on previous radio-telemetry and camera-trap studies of tiger movements and home ranges [17, 18].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Areas outside of this domain contained land-use formations unlikely to be occupied by resident tigers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We fitted two SCR models to the detection times of individual tigers observed in the spatial array of cameras.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  One model used a time-dependent binary indicator z(t) to specify whether each detection occurred during the daytime (i.e., between sunrise and sunset (z(t) = 1)) or during the nighttime (z(t) = 0).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We obtained sunrise and sunset times for each day of sampling from an online application http://aa.usno.navy.mil/data/docs/RS_OneYear.php developed by the Astronomical Applications Department of the United States Naval Observatory.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We also fitted a restricted model wherein detection rate was assumed to be constant at each camera location, as described in Model description.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This model did not require the detection times and was fitted using only the matrix of observed detection frequencies.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Neither of these SCR models included spatial covariates of tiger density or trap-specific covariates of tiger detection rate; however, we computed estimates of the spatial distribution of individual activity centers and tiger abundances using the full (unrestricted) model and a 500 m Ã— 500 m grid of the spatial domain.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The R source code [20] and data needed to fit these models are available in S2 Appendix.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Simulation_study  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We conducted a simulation study to examine the performance of our model when analyzing data sets of greater complexity than the tiger data.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In this study we simulated values of one spatially varying covariate v(s) of individual density and one spatially varying covariate w(s) of baseline detection rate over a square region B (Fig 4).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S3 Appendix contains a description of how values of each covariate were computed as a function of location s. The expected density of individuals at location s was computed using (s) = exp(1.4 + 0.8v(s)).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Given these parameter values, the expected number of individuals in region B was 150.2 (= (B)).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We assumed that individuals living in B may have been detected within a square array of 100 evenly-spaced traps located at the center of the region (Fig 4).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The baseline detection rate of individuals at trap location xk was computed using k = exp(0.7 + 1.0w(xk)).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Given these parameter values, baseline detection rates ranged from 0.41 to 0.61 detections per unit time among the K = 100 trap locations.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We assumed each trap operated continuously for 30 units of time.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We also assumed equal amounts of daytime and nighttime during this period and that each traps baseline detection rate during the day was about 37% of the rate at night.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In other words, we assumed  = 1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We assumed the parameter  was 0.4 to ensure that individuals in proximity to the trapping array were likely to be detected at multiple traps.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We fitted the full model and the restricted model to each of 300 data sets that were simulated independently.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Each model was fitted to a data set by running the MCMC algorithm (S1 Appendix) for 1200 iterations and by estimating posterior summaries from the final 1000 iterations of the Markov chain.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We examined the performance of these models by comparing the average of the estimated posterior mean of each parameter to its true (data-generating) value.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We also compared the estimated coverage of a 95% credible interval for each parameter to the nominal 95% level.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  


Results  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Camera-trap_survey_of_tigers  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A total of 86 individual tigers were detected during the period of sampling, which included a per-camera average of 21.2 days of daytime and 23.4 days of nighttime.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Tigers were detected in a total of 355 capture events.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most of these detections occurred during the nighttime (Fig 5), providing evidence of nocturnal activity patterns of tigers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Table 1 contains estimates of the parameters of both full and restricted SCR models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Substantial differences in parameter estimates were obtained only among the parameters that specify encounter rates (0 and ).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In particular the estimate of  was significantly negative, indicating that baseline detection rates of tigers were about five times lower during the daytime (0.009 detections per day) than during the night (0.045 detections per day).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The expected density of tigers throughout our spatial domain was 11.3 individuals per 100 km2 (95% credible interval = 9.113.9).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Although spatially varying covariates of tiger density were not used to fit our SCR model, the estimated spatial distribution of tiger abundance (Fig 6) revealed some locations where tiger densities were much higher than average.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The estimate of the scale parameter  was 1.70 km (95% credible interval = 1.601.82).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Using a Gausian kernel, we estimate that each tiger occupied an area of about 54 km2 (= ), which slightly exceeds the home range expected for resident male tigers (50 km2) [18].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Simulation_study  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In our simulated data sets the average number of individuals living in region B (150.3) was very close to the expected population size (150.2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The average number of individuals detected at least once in the trapping array was 124.0.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Table 2 contains averages of each parameters posterior mean and coverage estimated by fitting the full and the restricted SCR models to each simulated data set.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The average of the estimated posterior mean was close to the true (data-generating) value of every parameter with one exception.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The average of the logit-scale, mean baseline encounter rate o estimated under the restricted model was substantially lower than the true value of this parameter.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, the coverage of credible intervals of 0 obtained by fitting the restricted model was substantially lower than the nominal 95% level.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The reason for these discrepancies are obvious: under the assumptions of the data-generating model, mean baseline encounter rates differed during daytime and nighttime, whereas under the assumptions of the restricted model these rates were assumed to be identical.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, under the restricted model estimates of 0 correspond to the logit-scale, mean baseline encounter rate averaged over daytime and nighttime periods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Apart from this difference, the operating characteristics of full and restricted models were nearly identical.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  


Discussion  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In this article we described a class of hierarchical models for estimating the spatial distribution and abundance of animals detected with continuous-time recorders.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In these models the spatial distribution of individual activity centers is assumed to be a realization of a spatial point process parameterized to include the effects of spatially varying covariates (e.g., habitat measurements).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, the sequence of detection times observed for each unique individual is assumed to follow a temporal point process during the continuous period of each cameras operation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This process is parameterized to include the effects of trap-specific covariates on each individuals baseline rate of detection so that factors influencing animal movements or behaviors can potentially be inferred.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The effects of behavior also can be specified using time-specific covariates, provided measurements of these covariates are available during the entire period of each traps operation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These novel aspects of our continuous-time SCR model provide a more realistic description of the nocturnal activities and movements of tigers and other species with similar habits.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Several components of our SCR model are also present in other SCR models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, the effects of spatially varying covariates on density of activity centers has been modeled in some SCR models using a multinomial distribution to specify a discrete representation of the locations expected to be occupied by each individual ([2, 21], chapter 11).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A similar approach was used to specify each individuals utilization of space with a multinomial resource selection function ([2, 22], chapter 13).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This allowed [22] to establish a technical equivalence between an individuals resource selection and encounter probability.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Specifically, they showed that specifying the baseline rate of detection of an individual as a function of trap- and location-specific covariates (as in our SCR model) implies a particular resource selection function.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, if resource selection occurs it can be estimated from the effects of trap- and location-specific covariates on each individuals baseline rate of detection.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Our SCR model also shares many of the features of the continuous-time SCR model of [6].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, in that model the distribution of detection times was parameterized using a hazard function hk(t, xi; ), wherein xi denotes the activity center of individual i and k indexes the trap.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, our continuous-time SCR model uses the intensity function (t, si, xk) (defined in Eq (3)) of an inhomogenous Poisson process to specify the distribution of detection times for an individual with activity center si.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Both models allow an individuals baseline rate of detection to be modeled using trap-specific covariates (specified using log-linear formulations of  [6] or of k (our SCR model)).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The primary difference between our continuous-time SCR model and that of [6] lies in the treatment of individual activity centers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The model of [6] was used primarily to estimate model parameters, in particular the parameters that specify population density.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Parameters were estimated by maximizing the marginal likelihood function obtained by integrating individual activity centers from the joint probability density of the data and the activity centers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In contrast, our SCR model was fitted using a Bayesian approach that retains the individual activity centers as formal parameters; therefore, our model can be used to estimate the spatial distribution of individual activity centers, including valid estimates of their uncertainties.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As such, our SCR model essentially provides a species distribution model that allows a map of the distribution of abundance (or density) of individual activity centers to be estimated.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Moreover, if spatial covariates of individual density are unknown or unavailable, our SCR model can still be used to reveal spatial variation in the distribution of individual activity centers, as was evident in our analysis of the tiger data.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Given the cost-effectiveness and widespread use of camera-trap surveys, we anticipate increasing use of continuous-time SCR models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Unlike SCR models based on discrete periods of observation, continuous-time models are more accurate representations of the actual sampling process when camera traps or other types of continous-time recorders are used.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Continuous-time SCR models use all of the data, which may be advantageous in surveys of species at high density, where individuals are observed many times.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  An additional benefit of these models is their ability to specify aspects of the detection process more accurately when there are temporal variations in the activity of individual animals.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In our analysis of the tiger data, we illustrated use of temporal covariates to model nocturnal behavior; however, semiparametric models of temporal patterns (e.g., splines or other basis functions of time) also can be used in circumstances where covariates of detection are unknown or unavailable.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A challenging, but important, area of further research is the extension of continuous-time SCR models for estimating population dynamics.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Such open models have been developed for discrete-time SCR models ([2, 23], chapter 16).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similar approaches may provide useful extensions of continuous-time SCR models.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We plan to address this issue in ongoing work.  #@NEW_LINE#@#
