article id="http://dx.doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1722137115"  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
title  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Stable iridium dinuclear heterogeneous catalysts supported on metal-oxide substrate for solar water oxidation  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Significance  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
While research on single-atom catalysts (SACs) is arguably mature, rare work has been done on atomically dispersed catalyst featuring two atoms.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We synthesized Ir dinuclear heterogeneous catalyst in a facile photochemical way.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It exhibits outstanding stability and high activity toward water oxidation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The significance of this work can also be appreciated from the catalysis perspective.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A grand challenge in heterogeneous catalysis is how to understand the detailed mechanisms at the molecular level, because the most active heterogeneous catalysts are often poorly defined in their atomic structures.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our finding is built upon recent advances aimed at studying SACs but takes a crucial step forward.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It provides a material platform to study reactions that would require more than one active site.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Abstract  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Atomically dispersed catalysts refer to substrate-supported heterogeneous catalysts featuring one or a few active metal atoms that are separated from one another.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  They represent an important class of materials ranging from single-atom catalysts (SACs) and nanoparticles (NPs).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  While SACs and NPs have been extensively reported, catalysts featuring a few atoms with well-defined structures are poorly studied.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The difficulty in synthesizing such structures has been a critical challenge.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Here we report a facile photochemical method that produces catalytic centers consisting of two Ir metal cations, bridged by O and stably bound to a support.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Direct evidence unambiguously supporting the dinuclear nature of the catalysts anchored on -Fe2O3 is obtained by aberration-corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (AC-STEM).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Experimental and computational results further reveal that the threefold hollow binding sites on the OH-terminated surface of -Fe2O3 anchor the catalysts to provide outstanding stability against detachment or aggregation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The resulting catalysts exhibit high activities toward H2O photooxidation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Results  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Synthesis_Strategy_for_Ir_DHCs  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Building upon our previous successes in immobilizing homogeneous water oxidation catalysts (Ir homodimer) (19), we designed the synthesis of Ir DHCs as shown in Fig 1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The key step added to this work is the photochemical treatment, which was implemented to remove the organic ligands.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The characterization of the -Fe2O3 substrate and the resulting catalysts at each stage are presented in SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S1S3.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In addition to structure results to be presented below, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (SI Appendix, Fig S2) and electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS) (SI Appendix, Fig S3) clearly support that there is no N signal after the photochemical treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This provides strong evidence that the pyridyl ligands in the precursor have been removed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Direct_Structure_Characterization_of_Ir_DHCs  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The most direct evidence that proves we have obtained a dinuclear structure of Ir atoms that are not in direct contact but connected by an oxygen bridge is from the high-angle annular dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) data (Fig 2 A and B and SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S4S11).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The bright two-point features in Fig 2A were identified as Ir DHCs, and the lighter gray points in the background were ascribed to Fe atoms.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Tilting experiments confirmed that the Ir atoms are on top of the Fe columns (SI Appendix, Fig S7).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Nevertheless, we caution that we could not fully rule out the existence of other species not aligned with the Fe columns by STEM characterization alone.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Spectroscopic evidence lent additional support on this front (vide infra).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The ultrahigh signal-to-noise ratio was enabled by the high-Z contrast of Ir and Fe.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Importantly, few other components, such as Ir SACs or Ir NPs, were observed within the viewing field (more discussions on the distribution in SI Appendix, Fig S12), which is critical to this work because the SACs and NPs would otherwise complicate the mechanistic understanding of water oxidation by Ir DHCs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To further study the atomic arrangement of the Ir DHCs, a linescan for HAADF intensity was taken across the bright spots (Fig 2B), and the intensity data are plotted in Fig 2C.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The average distance between the two Ir atoms within a DHC was measured at 3 Å. Elemental mapping using STEMenergy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) (Fig 2 DG and SI Appendix, Fig S4) further supported that there were no large aggregates of Ir clusters.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The lack of Ir SACs or clusters was proven by spectroscopic characterization to be discussed below.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Taken as a whole, the unique two-atom arrangement and the lack of aggregated byproducts set the stage for detailed studies of DHCs as a new class of water-oxidation catalyst.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Our previous work has proven that the IrOIr arrangement within the Ir homodimer (Fig 1) is key to its high activity toward water oxidation (18, 19).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We therefore expect that IrOIr rather than IrIr arrangement is preserved in the Ir DHC.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Evidence from at least two complementary experimental techniques strongly supports our expectation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  First, we performed in situ CO diffuse reflectance infrared Fourier transform (DRIFT) spectroscopic characterization, and the purpose was to use CO as a probe to study the Ir atomic arrangement within the DHC.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  While the dinuclear nature of the structure is unambiguously confirmed by the HAADF data, an alternative arrangement to the proposed one could feature IrIr bonding.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the in situ CO DRIFT experiment, the two arrangements are expected to be readily distinguished by the characteristic absorption features at 1,850 cm1, which is indicative of the bridge adsorption of CO at the IrIr site (20).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Indeed, the broad peak at 1,850 cm1 was observed for Ir NPs control samples with known IrIr bonding (Fig 2I) but absent from Ir DHCs (Fig 2H).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Additionally, a strong dipoledipole coupling effect between adjacent CO probe molecules was evident in the control sample with Ir aggregates but was absent in Ir DHCs, further confirming the atomic dispersity of the latter.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  When the oxygen bridge of Ir DHC was broken to yield Ir SAC, we observed a redshift of the peaks characteristic of dicarbonyl species (SI Appendix, Fig S13).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Second, X-ray absorption spectroscopy data were acquired to reveal the coordination environment of the Ir center.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We see from SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S14S16 and Table S1 that the first shell coordination of Ir in DHCs is dominated by IrO (21), in excellent agreement with the proposed bonding of IrOIr but not IrIr.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Taken together, the microscopic and spectroscopic characterization unambiguously confirmed that we have obtained a dinuclear Ir catalyst that features discrete IrOIr rather than IrIr structures without organic ligands.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Here we further highlight the uniqueness of the synthesis method.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our previous experiments show that other treatments such as thermal annealing (19) or electrochemical decomposition (22) lead to obvious Ir aggregation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Only by photochemical treatments could we achieve the DHC structure in the high yield ( greater than 80%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Determination_of_Binding_Sites_by_Density-Functional_Theory_and_HAADF_Simulation  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The strong metalsupport interaction is crucial to maintaining the catalytic structure under catalytic operation conditions for many systems (4).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  How SACs can be anchored onto supporting substrates by defect sites has been demonstrated on reducible metal oxides (23).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Nevertheless, it would be a significant challenge to rely on the randomly distributed defect sites as the binding sites for DHCs that feature well-defined and uniform MOM arrangements.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To understand how our Ir DHCs bind to the -Fe2O3 support, we carried out density-functional theory (DFT) calculations.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our strategy was to construct hydroxyl-terminated -Fe2O3 (001) surfaces, as shown in Fig 3A.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Previous studies have shown that hydroxyl termination is the most stable in the presence of gas-phase H2O (24).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The optimized surface structures suggested that the IrOIr structure is stabilized by substrate O, H2O, and OH ligands (Fig 3 C and D).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Taken together, the two Ir atoms in the resulting DHC are bound by five surface O atoms, each Ir atom occupying a threefold hollow site on the OH-terminated -Fe2O3 surface (SI Appendix, Fig S18).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Importantly, the model built here allowed us to simulate the expected HAADF image under the experimental condition (Fig 3E), which is in excellent agreement with the experimental data in terms of the Ir atomic location (Fig 3A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, the simulated intensity linescan data reproduced the experimental ones as well, illustrating the accuracy of the interatomic distance in IrOIr site (Fig 3 B and F).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We note that the data presented here represent a typical example of one facet of -Fe2O3 (001), which provides a basis for more comprehensive future studies of other facets.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Catalytic_Characterization_of_Ir_DHCs  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Photoelectrochemical (PEC) characterization was next carried out to study the catalytic activity of the Ir DHC following previously developed protocols.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The Ir-based catalysts are known to be most active under acidic conditions (19).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As such, it is desirable to study them at low pHs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The support (-Fe2O3), however, would be dissolved by acids, leading to possible detachment of Ir that would undermine efforts to study its inherent catalytic activity and stability.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For these reasons, we optimized the test conditions at a pH of 6.0.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As shown in Fig 4A, compared with control samples with only -Fe2O3 support (bare in Fig 4A), the addition of Ir catalysts improved the overall performance by shifting the polarization curves toward the cathodic direction.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Previous thermodynamic and kinetic studies have revealed that such a shift may be due to either improved charge separation within the support or better charge-transfer kinetics or a combination of both (25, 26).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Given that the surface coverage of Ir DHCs on -Fe2O3 is sparse, we strongly believe faster kinetics is the true reason for such a shift.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The understanding is consistent with our previous kinetic studies (25).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The per-atom turnover frequency (TOF) of Ir DHCs at 1.23 V (vs. reversible hydrogen electrode, RHE) is 2.6 and 5 times higher than the corresponding Ir SACs (SI Appendix, Fig S20) and Ir NPs (SI Appendix, Fig S21), respectively, at the same potential.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In addition, compared with similar systems that feature Ir-based water photooxidation reactions (22, 25, 2729), the Ir DHC on -Fe2O3 features the higher normalized TOFs (SI Appendix, Table S3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  That the sample with Ir DHCs exhibited the high activity attests to its functionality as an effective water-oxidation catalyst despite the fact that the organic ligands of the molecular precursor have been removed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Proposed_Catalytic_Mechanism_by_DFT_Calculations  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Inspired by our previous studies on the Ir homogeneous catalysts (18) (SI Appendix, Fig S22), we propose a mechanism that involves multiple proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) steps by DFT calculations using the model built in Fig 3 C and D. Ir DHCs and homodimer molecules (18) share similar IrIV(OH2)OIrIV(OHx) structural motifs (x = 2 for Ir homodimer and x = 1 for Ir DHCs).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, it is reasonable to assume that the water-oxidation mechanism of Ir DHCs is similar to their homogeneous molecular analogs, albeit in the absence of the organic supporting ligands.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As shown in Fig 5A, Ir DHCs are proposed to undergo three PCET processes to build enough oxidation potential to oxidize H2O and produce Ir-OOH (steps E to F).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The free-energy changes of the three PCET steps are 1.06, 1.37, and 1.21 eV, respectively, at U = 0 V (U is the applied potential; Fig 5A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A substrate H2O molecule binds to the Ir DHC with a free-energy increase of 0.44 eV, which then undergoes nucleophilic attack to form hydrogen peroxide with a free-energy increase of 0.36 eV.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The oxidation of Ir-OOH intermediate F is easy to proceed through a PCET, requiring only 0.75 eV.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The subsequent O2 release is driven by the following substrate H2O binding.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  By comparing the free-energy changes under different applied potentials, an overpotential as low as 0.14 V is enough for the overall four-electron oxidation to be thermodynamically downhill.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The calculated overpotential indicates that the Ir DHC is an efficient catalyst for water oxidation, consistent with the experimentally observed low onset potential of the Ir DHC/-Fe2O3 (Von = 0.55 V, corresponding to an applied potential of 1.35 V by assuming a photovoltage of 0.8 V by -Fe2O3) (26).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
As an important control experiment, we carried out DFT calculations to compare the catalytic steps between the Ir DHC and SAC (Fig 5 and SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S23 and S24).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The atomic structure of Ir SAC was built by placing one Ir atom in one threefold O site.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The resting state of Ir SAC was identified as an Ir (IV) bound with an OH and two H2O ligands (Fig 5B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For water oxidation, Ir SAC first undergoes PCET processes twice, which is similar to the first two steps of water oxidation by Ir DHC.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The key difference between the two catalysts lies in the next step.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Whereas Ir DHC undergoes another PCET (steps C to D in Fig 5A) before forming the OO bond, Ir SAC has to bind to a H2O substrate and form the OO bond first (steps C to E in Fig 5B) before the third PCET.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This is because direct oxidation of Ir SAC after the second PCET step would require too high an energy (more than 1.8 eV).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  By comparison, the availability of another Ir atom nearby in Ir DHC reduces the energy need to 1.2 eV.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We note that more accurate calculations of the energy need for catalytic steps would require the inclusion of the supporting substrate (-Fe2O3) and solvation effects, which is beyond the scope of the present work.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our results nonetheless highlight the benefits of the synergistic effects between a dinuclear catalytic site (Ir DHC) over a mononuclear site (Ir SAC).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The understanding aligns with recent experiment observations that a dinuclear site may be more active toward water oxidation (30, 31).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We emphasize that the well-defined structure of the Ir DHC is key to our understanding reported here, which permits the construction of mechanistic models for detailed studies of the processes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Stability_Characterization_of_Ir_DHCs  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We characterized the stability of Ir DHCs in two different settings.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  First, the chronoamperometry data of Ir DHCs on -Fe2O3 for the first 10 h were compared with other Ir catalysts.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  No measurable degradation was observed (Fig 4B), suggesting that under PEC conditions the Ir DHC on -Fe2O3 is stable.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The stability is better than Ir SACs and Ir NPs (SI Appendix, Fig S25).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) characterization indicated that no Ir was detected in the electrolytes of the Ir DHCs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In addition, the key features in the data of STEM-EDS, in situ DRIFTS, and XPS by Ir DHCs after PEC test are similar to those by fresh samples, indicating that there was no aggregation (SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S26S28).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Second, the Ir DHCs were subjected to focused electron beam irradiation under HAADF-STEM conditions; the purpose was to observe the inherent thermal stability of Ir DHCs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As seen in Fig 4 CE, no aggregation or detachment was observed for up to 1 min of irradiation, which is longer than the reported SAC systems under the similar condition (32).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The O3-binding site offered by the -Fe2O3 substrate has proven critical to the formation and stability of Ir DHCs (Fig 3 C and D and SI Appendix, Fig S18).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We envision, however, that the binding is not unique to the chemical nature of the -Fe2O3 substrate.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As long as similar binding sites are available, Ir DHC structures with comparable stabilities and catalytic activities are expected on other supporting substrates.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  


Conclusion  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Heterogeneous catalysts with active moieties that are well defined in their atomic and/or molecular structures are expected to play important roles in the development of catalysis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The main challenge of this research direction lies in the synthesis and characterization of such catalysts.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Within this context, the Ir DHCs reported here represent a significant advance.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Not only are Ir DHCs a heterogeneous catalyst featuring two active-metal atoms, their structures are also well characterized at the atomic level.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The atomic arrangement of the catalytic center (IrOIr), which is key to the water-oxidation activities, is preserved by the strong binding provided by the supporting substrate.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The resulting Ir DHC exhibits outstanding stability against aggregation or detachment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It shows high activity toward water oxidation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The finding is built upon recent advances aimed at synthesizing and studying SACs but takes a crucial step forward.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It presents opportunities to study the detailed mechanisms of heterogeneous catalysis involving multiple but individually separated active atoms, which was carried out using DFT in this article.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The insights are expected to contribute to the design and optimization of heterogeneous catalysts and electrocatalysts.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Materials_and_Methods  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Preparation_of_Ir_Dinuclear_Catalyst_on_-Fe2O3_(Ir_DHC)_and_Loading_Estimate  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Preparation_of_Ir_Single-Atom_Catalysts_on_-Fe2O3_(Ir_SAC)  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Similar to the preparation of Ir DHC, Ir SAC was obtained by carrying out step 2, the photochemical treatments for 40 min.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The loading of Ir SAC is the same as the loading of Ir DHC sample, because no Ir evaporates during the photochemical treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Preparation_of_Ir-Based_Nanoparticles_on_-Fe2O3_(Ir_NP)  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

STEM  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
All samples were scraped from the F-doped tin oxide electrode and collected on Lacey carbon grids.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  HAADF-STEM images (Fig 2 DG and SI Appendix, Fig S1) were collected using a double-aberration-corrected FEI Titan3 G2 60300 instrument operating at 300 kV.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The semiangle of the probe-forming aperture was 22 mrad.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The inner and outer semiangles of the HAADF detector were 80 and 200 mrad, respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A probe current of 76 pA and dwelling time of 16 s per pixel were chosen for desirable signal-to-noise ratios.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  EDS was performed on a Super-X EDS system consisting of four Bruker silicon drift detectors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  During EDS mapping, sample drifting was corrected by a simultaneous image collector.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The Fe K edge, O K edge, and Ir M edge were used for elemental mapping.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  EELS were collected with a GIF quantum EELS spectrometer; a standard power-law background subtraction was utilized to remove the background.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  2 A and B, 3A, and 4 CE and SI Appendix, Figs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S4S6 and S9 were collected on an FEI Titan G2 80200 Crewley ChemiSTEM microscope, which was equipped with a high-brightness field-emission gun, a probe spherical aberration (Cs) corrector, and a super-X EDS system (33).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The semiconvergence angle for imaging was 24.7 mrad, while the collection semiangle was 70200 mrad.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A higher beam current of 280 pA was used with a longer dwell time of 0.1 ms per pixel and repeated 200× for atomic-resolution EDXS mapping.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Spatial drifting was corrected using a simultaneous image collector.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All high-resolution HAADF images shown in this work are raw data without any postfiltering.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

In_Situ_DRIFTS  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In situ DRIFTS measurements were performed using a Nicolet iS50 FTIR spectrometer equipped with a DTGS KBr detector and a Harrick praying mantis HVC-DRP4 cell equipped with KBr windows (34).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  After each sample was placed in the cell, it was heated to 105 °C under pure He for at least 15 min to remove physically adsorbed H2O and was then gradually cooled to room temperature.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Ir NPs/-Fe2O3 prepared by method 1 was further treated under 20% H2 at 150 °C for 2 h to remove potential thin oxide surface coatings.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Background spectra were then recorded at room temperature.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Subsequently, the gas flow was changed to CO for 15 min and was changed back to He to avoid possible interference of gaseous CO to the spectra.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  DRIFT spectra were recorded in the CO absorption region, 2,2001,800 cm1 after varying the He purging time (5, 10, 40, and 70 min).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The resolution was selected at 4 cm1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Sixty-four scans were recorded for each spectrum shown in the work.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

PEC_Characterization  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
PEC measurements were carried out using a potentiostat (Modulab XM equipped with Modulab XM ECS software).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The light source was a solar simulator (100 mW/cm2, Solarlight model 16S-300-M Air Mass Solar Simulator, AM 1.5).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Ir DHC/-Fe2O3, Ir SAC/-Fe2O3, Ir NPs/-Fe2O3, and -Fe2O3 were used as the working electrodes, with an SCE electrode as the reference electrode, and a Pt wire used as the counterelectrode.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The electrolyte was a 0.1 M KNO3 solution with the pH adjusted to 6.0.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The potential was corrected to the RHE scale by the Nernst equation (ERHE = ESCE + 0.059 pH + 0.241).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For linear sweep voltammetry data, the scan rate was 20 mV/s.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Computational_Details  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We used the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) for all periodic boundary calculations (35).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Projector augmented plane-wave method (36) together with the PerdewBurkeErnzerhof exchange-correlation functional (37) were employed to describe the electronion interactions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A cutoff of 450 eV was chosen for the plane-wave basis set in all calculations.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A 5 × 5 × 5 MonckhorstPack-type k-point grid was chosen for the optimization of bulk Fe2O3.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The Gaussian smear method was used for Fe2O3.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The  value was chosen to be 0.1 eV.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The energy convergence criterion was set to be 104 eV per unit cell and the geometry convergence criterion was set to be 103 eV per unit cell for energy difference between two consecutive ionic steps.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Additional details of the calculations are available in SI Appendix.  #@NEW_LINE#@#
