article id="http://dx.doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1523199113"  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
title  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Comparative genome-scale modelling of Staphylococcus aureus strains identifies strain-specific metabolic capabilities linked to pathogenicity  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Significance  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Comparative analysis of multiple strains within a species is a powerful way to uncover pathoadaptive genetic acquisitions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Hundreds of genome sequences are now available for the human pathogen Staphylococcus aureus, mostly known for its antibiotic-resistant variants that threaten the emergence of panresistant superbugs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In this study, genome-scale models of metabolism are used to analyze the shared and unique metabolic capabilities of this pathogen and its strain-specific variants.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The models are used to distinguish S. aureus strains responsible for severe infections based solely on growth capabilities and presence of different virulence factors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The results identify metabolic similarities and differences between S. aureus strains that provide insights into the epidemiology of S. aureus and may help to combat its spread.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Abstract  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Staphylococcus aureus is a preeminent bacterial pathogen capable of colonizing diverse ecological niches within its human host.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We describe here the pangenome of S. aureus based on analysis of genome sequences from 64 strains of S. aureus spanning a range of ecological niches, host types, and antibiotic resistance profiles.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Based on this set, S. aureus is expected to have an open pangenome composed of 7,411 genes and a core genome composed of 1,441 genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Metabolism was highly conserved in this core genome; however, differences were identified in amino acid and nucleotide biosynthesis pathways between the strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Genome-scale models (GEMs) of metabolism were constructed for the 64 strains of S. aureus.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These GEMs enabled a systems approach to characterizing the core metabolic and panmetabolic capabilities of the S. aureus species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All models were predicted to be auxotrophic for the vitamins niacin (vitamin B3) and thiamin (vitamin B1), whereas strain-specific auxotrophies were predicted for riboflavin (vitamin B2), guanosine, leucine, methionine, and cysteine, among others.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  GEMs were used to systematically analyze growth capabilities in more than 300 different growth-supporting environments.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The results identified metabolic capabilities linked to pathogenic traits and virulence acquisitions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Such traits can be used to differentiate strains responsible for mild vs. severe infections and preference for hosts (e.g., animals vs. humans).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Genome-scale analysis of multiple strains of a species can thus be used to identify metabolic determinants of virulence and increase our understanding of why certain strains of this deadly pathogen have spread rapidly throughout the world.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Results  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Characteristics_of_the_S_aureus_Core_Genome_and_Pangenome  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A set of publicly available S. aureus genome sequences was downloaded from the NCBI including 48 completely assembled genomes (16).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  From this set, a phylogenetic tree was constructed using the concatenated sequence of seven conserved housekeeping genes (arcC, aroE, glpF, gmk, pta, tpi, and yqiL) commonly used to define clonal complexes in clinical studies of S. aureus using the MLST approach (Fig 1A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The most distantly related strain was the Australian S. aureus isolate MSHR1132 belonging to the clonal complex 75 lineage (17).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Next, a representative set of 64 S. aureus strains (Dataset S1) were filtered from this larger dataset based on four criteria: (i) drug resistance [MRSA, methicillin-sensitive S. aureus (MSSA), vancomycin-resistant S. aureus (VRSA), and vancomycin-intermediate S. aureus (VISA)] (Fig 1B), (ii) host specificity (human vs. animal) (Fig 1C), and (iii) epidemiological source [community-associated MRSA (CA-MRSA), healthcare-acquired MRSA (HA-MRSA), and livestock-associated MRSA (LA-MRSA)] (Fig 1D).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, the topology of the tree coupled with clinical and epidemiological phenotypic information allowed for development of a heterogeneous dataset spanning representative strains of the S. aureus species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We used these 64 strains to construct the S. aureus pangenome that can be used to describe this bacterial species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The pangenome is divided into three sections: (i) the core genome (the set of genes shared by virtually all strains in a species), (ii) the accessory genome (the set of genes present in some, but not all, representatives of a species), and (iii) the unique genome (genes unique to individual members of the species).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Because the pangenome is made up of thousands of genes, it offers a much higher resolution for strain typing compared with genotyping techniques such as MLST (18).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The 64 S. aureus strains examined yielded a pangenome size of 7,457 genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Based on this dataset, the core genome is composed of 1,441 genes, the accessory genome is composed of 2,871 genes, and the unique genome is composed of 3,145 genes (Fig 2A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Functional annotation of genes in the pangenome performed using the Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COG) database (19) revealed a unique distribution of functional categories among the three different pangenome sets.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  When genes were classified into metabolic and nonmetabolic, the largest fraction of the core genome consisted of genes with metabolic functions (58%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These included those involved in central metabolism including glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, the pentose phosphate pathway, and TCA cycle.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A much lower fraction of metabolic gene content is observed in the accessory and unique genome (33% and 18%, respectively).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The size of the pangenome and its increase in size upon addition of new strains can be used to predict the future rate of discovery of novel genes in a species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We used a sampling approach to obtain a set of randomly permuted S. aureus pangenomes (with specific breakdowns of total genes, shared genes, and new genes).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  By fitting a double exponential decay function to the number of shared genes, we estimated the S. aureus core genome to have 1,425 genes (Fig 3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Discovery of new genes and count of total genes were used to obtain fits to the Heaps law function, resulting in  values of 0.58 and 0.31, respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The  parameter determines the behavior of the curve.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For  values 0, the function has no asymptote, indicating that the S. aureus pangenome repertoire is likely to grow indefinitely as more strains are sequenced.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Size_and_Content_of_the_Core_Genome_Provide_Insight_into_Essential_S_aureus_Capabilities  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The average S. aureus genome encodes 2,800 genes; therefore, the size of the core genome represents a high portion (56%, on average) of each S. aureus genome (Fig 2B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This portion is particularly high compared with other organisms including Clostridium difficile (core represents 25% of the average genome) (20) and Escherichia coli (core represents 40% of the average genome) (21).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The fact that each S. aureus strain has such a high portion of shared genes can be interpreted as a direct consequence of the proposed clonal structure for the species (22).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The majority of the 1,441 core genes (shared by all 64 S. aureus strains examined) are involved in housekeeping processes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These include nonmetabolic functions such as transcription (15%), translation, ribosomal structure and biogenesis (14%), and RNA processing and modification (7%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A core set of 239 genes functionally annotated to be involved in transcription and translation was found in all S. aureus strains examined (Fig S1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A detailed discussion of these genes is provided in SI Text.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of these 239 conserved genes, 94 and 87 are known to be experimentally essential on LB media in E. coli (23) and Bacillus subtilis (24), respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, inhibitors of these proteins may be good targets for antibiotic therapies against S. aureus.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Genes involved in metabolic functions were also highly conserved.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Such functions included amino acid transport and metabolism (11%), carbohydrate transport and metabolism (7%), coenzyme transport and metabolism (4%), cell wall and membrane biosynthesis (5%), and energy production and conversion (12%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Genome-scale network reconstructions for each of the 64 strains were built to perform an integrated analysis of the function of these metabolic genes (see below).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Together, genes involved in metabolic, transcription, and translation processes make up 75% of the S. aureus core genome.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Relative to the core genome, widely different functional assignments are present for those genes in the accessory and unique genomes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The accessory genome has a large portion of genes associated with mobile genetic elements such as transposons and bacteriophages [replication, recombination, and repair (24%)] or those with nonmetabolic functions including defense mechanisms (5%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Metabolic functions were also included in the accessory genome, including those related to amino acid metabolism (8%), inorganic ion transport (7%), and carbohydrate metabolism (6%).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The unique genome is heavily enriched in genes related to mobile elements (62%), with the other categories being poorly represented.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This high proportion of mobile elements in the unique genome is similar to other organisms, including E. coli, and indicates that horizontal gene transfer (HGT) has had a large effect on S. aureus evolution (2527).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Indeed, the archaic MRSA clone, thought to be the ancestor strain of MRSA in Europe, obtained its methicillin resistance because of the horizontal transfer of the mecA gene from an unknown source (1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Even strains in the same household have been shown to engage in HGT with other Staphylococcus species (28).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, analysis of all HGT events in different strains of S. aureus and their putative source can shed light on the evolution of this species (see SI Text for analysis of atypical genes and their putative transfer source).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Ancestral_Events_of_Gene_Loss_and_Acquisition_Affect_the_Pathogenicity_of_S_aureus_Strains  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We used the identified 1,441 core genes to produce an S. aureus phylogeny based on a concatenated sequence of the aligned genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The patterns of gene presence were used with this phylogeny based on the core genome to infer events of ancestral gene gains and losses using a parsimony approach (29).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This analysis allowed, for each internal node of the phylogenetic tree, us to compute (i) the number of gene gains and losses and (ii) the corresponding genetic repertoire.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In particular, we focused on the evolutionary events related to the virulence factors in each strain because they have profound implications on pathogenesis of different S. aureus clones.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The mapping of the events of gene gains and losses revealed that S. aureus strains have undergone extensive rearrangement of their genetic repertoire.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A large number of evolutionary events occurred both ancestrally and recently (Fig S2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, the ancestor of the ST228 lineage is characterized by a massive genome reduction (522 genes lost), whereas all of the LA-MRSA strains have recently acquired a large number of genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Considering that the S. aureus last common ancestor (Sa-LCA) comprised 2,673 ortholog groups, the species has acquired a large number of new genes (4,784) during its evolutionary history.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Known_S_aureus_Virulence_Factors_Are_Unequally_Distributed_Between_Core_and_Accessory_Genomes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To gain insight into the conservation of virulence factors across the S. aureus species we curated a set of known virulence factors (VFs) present in different strains based on literature and database searches (30).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We identified a total of 90 different VFs that were present in at least one of the 64 different S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 90 VFs, 35 were shared by all of the strains, forming a core set of VFs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Nine of the conserved VFs are cap8 genes (B, C, E, F, L, M, N, O, and P), which are involved in the synthesis of the polysaccharide capsule (PC).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Other conserved VFs included five involved in the production of two different cytotoxins, namely, the PantonValentine leukocidin (PVL), encoded by the genes lukS and lukF, and the gammahemolysin, encoded by hlgA, hlgB, and hlgC.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Four other conserved VFs encode iron-regulated proteins (isdA, isdC, isdE, and isdF) that bind to extracellular matrix components such as fibrinogen and fibronectin to promote cell adherence.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Among these, the isdA gene plays a role in the S. aureus iron acquisition system, important for S. aureus in vivo replication and disease pathogenesis (31).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Other canonical S. aureus virulence factors were highly conserved but were not found across all 64 strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Protein A (binds immunoglobin G to disrupt phagocytosis, encoded for by spa) was found in 90% of strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Alpha toxin (disrupts the membrane and enhances invasiveness, encoded for by hla) was found in 96% of strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Biosynthesis genes for the staphyloxanthin pigment (encoded for by crtMNPQ) were found in all but one of the strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This strain (MSHR1132) was specifically sequenced because of its lack of the yellow pigment (17).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Several other VFs were strain-specific.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, the Staphylococcal complement inhibitor (SCIN) protein (encoded by scn) was present in 48 of the 64 strains, and Chemotaxis inhibitory protein of staphylococci (CHIPS, encoded by chp) was present in 27 of the 64 strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The AGR quorum sensing system (regulates biofilm development, encoded for by agrABCD) was found in 25% of strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Meanwhile, the exfoliative toxin B was found only in a single strain (S. aureus 11819-97).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S. aureus MW2 was found to have the highest count of established virulence factors (79 VFs) followed by MSSA476 (74 VFs), Mu3, and Mu50 (70 VFs).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In contrast, the ST288 strains had the fewest number of VFs (53 total).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Metabolic_Models_Facilitate_Investigation_into_Genetic_Basis_of_Strain-Specific_Growth_Capabilities  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Because so much of the S. aureus core genome is dedicated to metabolic functions, the 64 S. aureus genome sequences were used to construct strain-specific genome-scale metabolic reconstructions (Dataset S2) that were used to compare gene, reaction, and metabolite content between the strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Each reconstruction serves as a comprehensive representation of the metabolic capabilities of an S. aureus strain.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Content shared among all reconstructions defines the core metabolic capabilities of the S. aureus species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, the metabolic capabilities of all strains were combined to define the full potential of metabolic capabilities for the S. aureus species, or its panmetabolic network (Fig 4A) (10).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The core metabolic network contained 700 metabolic genes that catalyze 1,222 reactions involving 1,257 metabolites.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Highly conserved metabolic subsystems across all S. aureus models include lipid metabolism, energy metabolism, glycan biosynthesis, and metabolism of polyketides and terpenoids.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Reactions involved in these metabolic subsystems were highly represented (95% conserved) in the core metabolic network.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  By contrast, only 80% of amino acid biosynthesis reactions were part of the core metabolic network.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Conserved amino acid biosynthesis pathways included those for valine, alanine, and serine biosynthesis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The core metabolic network also contained known metabolic virulence determinants including catalase, an enzyme that hydrolyzes hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen and that is used in the clinical laboratory to distinguish staphylococci from enterococci and streptococci.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Catalase production and oxidant resistance have been shown to be predisposing factors for nasal colonization and subsequent infection (32).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The panmetabolic reactions are composed of the union of different metabolic reactions found in all strains and thus indicate the pool of metabolic capabilities within a species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The S. aureus panmetabolic network contains 1,090 metabolic genes, 1,592 reactions, and 1,519 metabolites.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  About 45% of reactions in carbohydrate metabolism were not present in the core metabolic network.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, these reactions were not shared by all strains of S. aureus examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These formed the largest group of reactions present in the set difference between the core metabolic and panmetabolic networks (Fig 4B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A majority of these reactions are involved in alternate carbon metabolism, including catabolic pathways for unique niche-specific nutrients.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Amino acid metabolism was also disproportionately present in the panmetabolic network including biosynthesis pathways for leucine, arginine, and histidine metabolism, indicating that these capabilities may have been lost by several strains of S. aureus.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The conversion of metabolic network reconstructions into computable mathematical models enables computation of phenotypes in diverse nutrient environments based on the content of each reconstruction.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, the 64 S. aureus strain-specific reconstructed networks were converted into genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs) that were used to compute phenotypes in more than 300 unique, growth-supporting environmental conditions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A detailed biomass composition was defined (Table S1) and used to identify the distribution of metabolic fluxes leading to optimal growth.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Reactions belonging to the amino acid metabolism subsystem made up the majority of reactions in set difference between the core reactomes and panreactomes (Fig 4B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, we hypothesized that functional differences in amino acid biosynthesis capabilities of different strains of S. aureus may allow different strains to adapt to different nutritional environments.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To test this hypothesis, we simulated growth in silico for all 64 S. aureus GEMs on a variety of minimal media growth conditions, including a minimal growth media reported for S. aureus N315 (12).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The in silico growth analysis revealed that all 64 models growing in glucose minimal media require at least vitamins B1 (thiamin) and B3 (niacin) to be added to the media.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Some of the strains required more components to be added to the minimal media beyond just these (Table 1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The thiamin auxotrophy is due to a lack of the pathway that converts tyrosine to thiamin via tyrosine lyase and thiazole phosphate synthase.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The niacin auxotrophy is due to a lack of nicotinate-nucleotide diphosphorylase (EC 2.4.2.19).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These auxotrophies have been experimentally documented for S. aureus in the past (33).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
There were also several additional strain-specific auxotrophies with 90% of the models (55/64) unable to grow in the in silico glucose minimal media containing thiamin and niacin (Table S2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These models lacked the ability to synthesize additional compounds including the nucleotide guanine and the vitamin riboflavin as well as the amino acids leucine, arginine, histidine, tryptophan, phenylalanine, methionine, proline, and tyrosine (Table 1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Previous work has demonstrated that some strains of S. aureus are able to grow in minimal media devoid of amino acids after a long period of training or weaning away from amino acid addition (33, 34).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We experimentally confirmed that four strains (USA300, N315, 8325, and Mu50) could grow in minimal media supplemented with proline, serine leucine, threonine, thiamin, and niacin (Fig S3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S. aureus is known to have a complicated gene regulatory structure that may inhibit its growth on minimal media, despite the presence of biosynthetic pathways for these metabolites (12, 14).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Longer-term growth or training in the laboratory for these strains may allow for eventual growth in the minimal medium defined here (35).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

S_aureus_Strains_Can_Be_Differentiated_Using_GEM-Predicted_Metabolic_Capabilities_and_Presence_of_Virulence_Factors  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The 64 S. aureus GEMs were used to predict growth capabilities on alternative carbon, nitrogen, phosphorous, and sulfur sources by removing glucose, ammonia, sulfate, and phosphate from the in silico growth media and adding alternative sources one at a time.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Over 300 alternative nutrient sources were tested in aerobic and anaerobic conditions using flux balance analysis (FBA) (36) to assess whether each S. aureus strain grew in silico (Fig 5A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Overall, 238 nutrients were universally catabolized by all strains including glucose and glycerol as carbon sources and arginine as a nitrogen source.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Other nutrients were strain-specific, including dulcose (42 strains) and inosine (13 strains) as carbon sources and uracil and thymidine as nitrogen sources (42 strains each).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We specifically looked to see if the models were predicted to be able to use arginine for growth.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The arginine mobile catabolic element (ACME) is present in MRSA USA300 strains and has been shown to be a key element for its successful colonization (37).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We, however, found that the arginine catabolic capability was conserved across all S. aureus models due to the presence of a separate arc operon (encoded for by arcA-arcD) present in all strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This finding is consistent with previous work (38).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Despite this similarity, several other growth capabilities could be used to distinguish groups of strains (Fig 5).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, simulation results showed that the two S. aureus USA300 isolates were the only strains capable of using spermidine as a sole source of carbon and nitrogen due to the presence of spermidine acetyltransferase (encoded for by speG).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Spermidine is produced at high levels in areas of keratinocyte proliferation, inflammation, and wound healing (39), conditions under which S. aureus invades and causes skin infection.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We experimentally confirmed that only S. aureus USA300 could grow in TSB media supplemented with 6 mM of spermidine, whereas S. aureus strains Newman and NCTC8325 could not (Fig S4).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Another example of using model predictions to distinguish strains was made possible by combining the simulated growth results with presence/absence of virulence factors in each strain.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This combination allowed the formation of a classification schema that could be used to differentiate strains with a lifestyle that is livestock associated (LA) compared with those that were human associated (HA).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The classifier was capable of distinguishing strains with these two host preferences based on evaluation of only three factors: presence of staphylokinase precursor (sak), the ability to catabolize maltotriose (mlttr), and presence of IgG binding protein A precursor (sak) (Fig 5B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Interestingly, 100% of the sak positive strains were human-associated.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We also observed that although many of the strains from the same clonal complexes clustered near each other, some important differences arose.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, strains from the ST228 lineage appear to have lost several metabolic capabilities relative to others, causing two separate groups from this clonal complex to cluster apart from each other.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, GEM-predicted metabolic capabilities and presence of virulence factors can be used to classify strains of S. aureus based on lifestyle and preferred niche.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, the tools presented here are capable of offering more precise classification schemes than those used routinely in the clinic to type S. aureus strains today.  #@NEW_LINE#@#

Analysis_of_Atypical_S_aureus_Genes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To investigate the effect of HGT events on the S. aureus species, we searched for atypical genes within each strain by computing DNA composition (GC content) and codon use differences between core genes and pangenes (59).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A total of 4,277 and 1,788 atypical genes were identified with the average genome having 49 and 28 unique genes present based on GC content and codon bias, respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most of the identified atypical genes have no known COG functional class.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The phylogenetic origin of each atypical S. aureus gene was traced using the nr-database.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For each gene the best non-S. aureus hit was taken as the putative transfer source (PTS).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We hypothesized that more recent HGT events were indicated by genes with fewer S. aureus hits, i.e., those more similar to the query gene than the PTS.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We used this number as an index to estimate the ancestrality of the HGT events for each PTS, a measurement we term the ancestrality index (AI).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It was only possible to find a significant PTS for 25% of the atypical genes we identified.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Those PTS with an AI 2 mostly corresponded to HGT events that occurred at the genus level, whereas the majority of the more recent PTS (an AI 2) corresponded to HGT events at higher taxonomical levels (order, class, and phylum).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In this group we found a high representation of proteins such as hypothetical proteins, mobilization proteins, metal and antibiotic resistance genes, and virulence factors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We assessed the effect of HGT events in shaping the diversity within this species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Analysis of atypical genes showed how these are mostly derived from taxonomically related donors (i.e., representatives of the same species/genus), with a minor portion of genes coming from host-associated bacteria.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This finding suggests the presence of a taxonomical barrier limiting the amount of HGT in this species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The HGT events may be a major source of newly acquired antibiotic resistances.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, the number of unique genes per genome may identify strains more prone to HGT and hence more likely to acquire new functionalities.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Because virulence and antibiotic resistance genes are often involved in HGT events (42, 77), strains with a higher portion of unique genes (and thus more predisposed to exchange of exogenous DNA) may represent a major public health threat because these can develop virulent and/or multidrug-resistant phenotypes by horizontally acquiring corresponding gene cassettes (7880); however, we did not see any correlation between number of atypical genes and the number of identified virulence factors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

E-Gene_Conservation  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Most antibiotics not only target an organisms metabolic functions but also target the transcriptional and translational machinery of the target organism.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We compared the conservation of transcriptional and translational machinery within S. aureus strains across the species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Genes involved in these processes were curated from E. coli and B. subtilis because E. coli is the organism for which almost all components of transcription and translation machinery have been identified and experimentally characterized.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, because S. aureus is phylogenetically closer to B. subtilis (both are Firmicutes), additional proteins from B. subtilis were used to assemble this dataset.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Although B. subtilis homologs exist for most of the E. coli genes involved in transcription and translation, a few B. subtilis genes exist for which no homologs are found in the E. coli genome and vice versa.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Altogether, we selected 289 query genes of which 192 are shared between E. coli and B. subtilis, whereas 59 are unique to E. coli and 42 are unique to B. subtilis (Fig S3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The set included genes that encode functions for transcription, ribosome biogenesis, tRNA maturation and aminoacylation, and proteins and cofactors required for mRNA translation and RNA decay.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Using these experimentally validated genes as input (81), genes encoding proteins of the core transcription and translation machinery were predicted in the 64 S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A core set of 239 genes involved in transcription and translation was found in all S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The majority of genes coding for ribosomal proteins, aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases, translation factors, and several ribosome biogenesis/maturation enzymes are universally conserved in S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These same genes are essential in both E. coli and B. subtilis (8284) and other bacterial organisms (85, 86).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Conversely, 47 of the 289 genes were absent in all S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of genes absent in all S. aureus strains examined, 7 are present in both E. coli and B. subtilis, 33 are unique to E. coli, and 7 are unique to B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most of these missing genes encode functions in transcription, tRNA modifications, rRNA modifications, and RNA processing.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Next we examined genes present in some S. aureus strains but not others.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These included the ribonuclease RNE involved in RNA processing and the 50s ribosomal protein L33 (RPMGA).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These two proteins were conserved in 28 of the S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RMPGA was missing in 19 strains, and RNE was missing in 10 strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Although most of the S. aureus strains retained the two genes encoding L33a (RpmGa) and L33b (RpmGb), L33a was lost in 19 of the 64 strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  L33 is responsible for cellular ribosome heterogeneity and may generate specialized ribosomes in response to stress conditions and environmental changes (87).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These proteins could have evolved to fulfill specific nonessential innovation (88) and hence easily be lost in reductive evolutions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The L33 gene is also nonessential in E. coli and B. subtilis (23, 8991).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our analysis defines the minimal and conserved set of genes needed to encode functions that sustain protein synthesis in various S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A core set of 239 genes involved in transcription and translation were found in all S. aureus strains examined (Fig S1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of these, 183 were present in both E. coli and B. subtilis, whereas 23 were unique to E. coli, and 33 were unique to B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Ribosomal_Proteins__Aminoacyl-tRNA_Synthetases__Translation_Factors__and_Several_Ribosome_Biogenesis_Maturation_Enzymes_Are_Highly_Conserved_in_S_aureus  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Only one translation factor, ArfA, the alternative ribosome rescue factor A, was missing from all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It was shown recently that ribosomes stalled on nonstop mRNAs are rescued by dual mechanisms in E. coli: tmRNA mediated transtranslation [encoded for by SsrA and ArfA-mediated peptidyl-tRNA hydrolysis (92)].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  ArfA functions by recruiting release factor 2 (RF2) to release tRNA, and the presence of ArfA is essential in the absence of Ssra.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In addition to all of the S. aureus strains, ArfA is also missing in B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Thus, because they are missing the ArfA backup function, the SsrA function is essential in these organisms.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Certain drugs like tetracycline prevent the aminoacyl-tRNA from binding to the ribosomal subunit in prokaryotes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All S. aureus genomes analyzed encoded the complete set of aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases and protein cofactors required to charge all 20 canonical amino acids.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 33 aa-tRNA synthetases examined, 31 were conserved in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The two missing genes include SelA and SelD.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  SelA encodes selenocysteine synthase that catalyzes the conversion of serine to selenocysteine on serine-charged tRNASec.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  SelD encodes selenide, and water dikinase catalyzes the reaction that produces selenophosphate, the selenium donor for the biosynthesis of selenocysteine and modification of thiouridine to selenouridine in certain tRNAs (89).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Both genes are missing in B. subtilis and all of the S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Genes_Coding_for_Enzymes_Involved_in_Transcription__rRNA_and_Protein_Processing__RNA_or_Protein_Modification__and_Ribosome_Maturation_RNases_Are_Less_Conserved_in_S_aureus  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Of the 29 genes involved in transcription, 12 were absent in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 12 genes missing, 7 are also absent from B. subtilis including RpoE, RpoH, and RpoZ as well as FecL, DmsD, Mfd, and TorD.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RpoN was present in both E. coli and B. subtilis but lacking in all S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Finally, SigVWXZ were missing in all S. aureus strains, despite being present in B. subtilis indicating different transcriptional control strategies.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Out of the total 33 genes coding for rRNA modification enzymes in both E. coli and B. subtilis, 7 are absent in all strains of S. aureus examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of these, 4 ribosomal RNA large subunit methyltransferases are also missing in B. subtilis (RlmE, RlmF, RlmJ, and RlmM).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RlmA and RsmJ are found in both E. coli and B. subtilis but not S. aureus.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RsmJ mutants in E. coli are cold sensitive and show a growth defect at 16 Â°C.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 39 genes examined in ribosome assembly, 33 are present in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the genes missing, all five are also all absent in B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Of the 27 genes coding for RNases and related proteins, 9 were absent in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Eighteen were found in all of the strains analyzed, including two genes unique to E. coli (absent from B. subtilis, RNB and RNT).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RNases generally harbor broad, sometimes overlapping specificity with other RNases, making it difficult to determine their intrinsic essentiality.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Also, compared with the other categories of proteins analyzed above, the set of RNases in gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria are quite different, and some RNases are essential in one organism but not in the other (89, 93).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Furthermore, one gene was found to be differentially distributed in S. aureus strains, the RNA degradosome binding protein RnE.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RnE is found in E. coli but not B. subtilis and was found in 43 of the 64 S. aureus genomes analyzed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RnE is an endonuclease that cleaves single stranded regions of pre-RNA transcripts.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Its function is similar to the conserved RnjA and RnjB, two enzymes found only in gram-positive bacteria.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Ribosomal_Proteins  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The major function of a ribosomal protein is stabilization of the rRNA structure.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 60 genes encoding for r-proteins present in ribosomes of E. coli and or B. subtilis, 58 are present in the 53 S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Only one gene (RpsV/sra) encoding the ribosome associated protein S22/RpsV was missing in all S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This gene is also absent in B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It is nonessential in E. coli and codes for the substoichiometric component of the 30S ribosomal subunit.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Most of the strains tend to retain the two genes encoding L33a (RpmGa) and L33b (RpmGb), but L33a was lost in 19 of the 53 strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  L33 is known to be responsible for cellular ribosome heterogeneity, probably generating specialized ribosomes in response to stress conditions and environmental changes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These proteins could have evolved to fulfill specific nonessential innovations and hence could easily be lost in reductive evolutions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This gene is also nonessential in E. coli and B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Translation_Factors  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In addition to the core ribosomal components, protein synthesis requires several translation factors that ensure the speed and fidelity of translation as well as the functionality of the nascent polypeptide.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most of them are found in all S. aureus strains examined, illustrating the conservation of this bacterial apparatus in the bacterial world.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Only one translation factor, ArfA, the alternative ribosome rescue factor A, was missing from all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It was shown recently that ribosomes stalled on nonstop mRNAs are rescued by dual mechanisms in E. coli: tmRNA mediated transtranslation (encoded for by ssrA) and ArfA-mediated peptidyl-tRNA hydrolysis (55).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  ArfA functions by recruiting release factor 2 (RF2) to release tRNA and the presence of ArfA is essential in the absence of Ssra.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In addition to all of the S. aureus strains, ArfA is also missing in B. subtilis, making the SsrA function essential in these organisms.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Aminoacyl-tRNA_Synthetases  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Aminoacyl-tRNA (aa-tRNA or charged tRNA) is tRNA to which its cognate amino acid is chemically bonded (charged).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Certain drugs like tetracycline prevent the aminoacyl-tRNA from binding to the ribosomal subunit in prokaryotes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All S. aureus genomes analyzed encoded the complete set of aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases and protein cofactors required to charge all 20 canonical amino acids.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 33 aa-tRNA synthetases examined, 29 were conserved in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
SelA encodes selenocysteine synthase that catalyzes the conversion of serine to selenocysteine on serine-charged tRNASec.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  SelD encodes selenide, and water dikinase catalyzes the reaction that produces selenophosphate, the selenium donor for the biosynthesis of selenocysteine and modification of thiouridine to selenouridine in certain tRNAs (94).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Both genes are missing in B. subtilis and all of the S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Two other genes, glyQ and glyS, are present in B. subtilis but not conserved at the sequence level with their homologs in S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It was determined that glyS and glyQ are actually present in S. aureus at an equivalent position within a syntenic region that encodes a class-II glycyl-tRNA synthetase, similar to that encoded by Bacillus cereus (31).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These genes are also essential in S. aureus (95).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Transfer_RNA_Modification_Enzymes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Transfer RNA (tRNA) precursors are subject to enzymatic posttranscriptional modification at many positions of the base or ribose moieties.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These modifications stabilize the tRNA tertiary structure, introduce recognition determined and antideterminants toward RNA-interacting macromolecules, and fine-tune the decoding process at the level of both efficiency and fidelity.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 44 tRNA modification enzymes examined, 35 of the 44 are present in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All of the nine missing genes (cmoA, cmoB, mnmC, selU, tmcA, trmJ, truD, tsaA, and ttcA) all are also absent in B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, of the 35 genes present in all S. aureus strains, 6 are missing in B. subtilis but present in E. coli.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These genes are dusA, dusC/DUS2, gluQ, trmA, trmH, and truC and were possibly acquired in lateral gene transfer.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Ribosomal_RNA_Modification_Enzymes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Many bases and riboses of rRNAs are posttranscriptionally modified like in tRNAs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Most modifications are introduced during pre-rRNA maturation and ribosome assembly, and just a few are formed at the level of the 30S and 50S subparticles or of the entire 70S ribosome.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The conservation and clustering of modifications in the decoding center of the 30S subunit and in the peptidyl-transferase center of the 50S subunit attests their important roles in the translation process.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Out of the total 33 genes coding for rRNA modification enzymes in both E. coli and B. subtilis, only 7 are absent in all strains of S. aureus examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of these, 4 ribosomal RNA large subunit methyltransferases are also missing in B. subtilius (RlmE, RlmF, RlmJ, and RlmM).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RlmA and RsmJ are found in both E. coli and B. subtilis but not S. aureus.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RsmJ mutants in E. coli are cold sensitive and show a growth defect at 16C.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  YqxC is found in B. subtilis alone but not S. aureus; this gene encodes a FtsJ/Spb1/SPOUT-like 2-O-ribose RNA methyltransferase (81).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Ribosome_Assembly__Protein_Chaperones__Helicases__and_Protein_Modifications  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In bacteria, the assembly of r-proteins onto precursor rRNA scaffolds to form functional 30S and 50S subunits requires over a dozen assembly/stability factors as well as posttranslational protein modifications.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 39 genes examined, 33 are present in all S. aureus strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the missing genes, all five are also all absent in B. subtilis.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These included rimF, rimK, ycaO, yibL, and yjgA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In E. coli, RimK is an l-glutamate ligase that catalyzes posttranslational addition of up to four C-terminal glutamate residues to 30S ribosomal subunit protein S6, and a mutation in rimK was found to confer neomycin and kanamycin resistance (nek).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

RNA_Processing_Ribonucleases  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The various RNA components of the bacterial translation machinery are synthesized as longer precursor molecules that require subsequent processing steps, sizing, and 5 or 3 end trimming by a combination of endonucleases and exonucleases.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These ribonucleases also play an important role in controlling the activity and quality of the translation machinery and the regulation of gene expression by RNA turnover.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RNases generally harbor broad, sometimes overlapping specificity with other RNases, making difficult to determine their intrinsic essentiality.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Also, at variance with the six other categories of proteins analyzed above, the set of RNases in gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria are quite different; some RNases are essential in one organism but not in the other.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 27 genes coding for RNases and related proteins we analyzed, 18 were found in all of the strains analyzed, including 2 genes unique to E. coli (absent from S. aureus) (RNB and RNT).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Seven genes were absent from all S. aureus strains examined: bsn, nrnB, orn, rna, rnd, and rng; of these, only rnhA, bsn, and nrna are found in B. subtilis, and the others are unique to E. coli.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Bsn (YurI in B. subtilis) is an RNase of gram-positive bacteria and hydrolyzes RNA nonspecifically into oligonucleotides with 5-phosphate therefore likely playing a role in nutrient cycling.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Whereas E. coli and other gram-negative bacteria possess only one essential oligoribonuclease (nano-RNase, Orn) for degrading oligoribonucleotides of two to five residues sin length, B. subtilis possesses two nonorthologous nano-RNases with redundant specificity: NrnA (Ytql) and NrnB (YngD).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All S. aureus strains lack the nrnA gene.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  RnhA is one of the multivariant Ribonucleases H (HI = RnHA, HII = RnHB, and HIII = RnHC) that cleave RNA of RNADNA hybrids.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Their primary function is to prevent aberrant DNA replication at sites other than oriC.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All S. aureus strains examined lost RnHA (HI), but the function is redundant with the other two genes present in all strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Furthermore, one gene was found to be unevenly distributed in S. aureus strains: rnE.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This gene is present in E. coli but not B. subtilis and was found in 43 of the 53 genomes analyzed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It encodes an endonuclease that cleaves single-stranded regions of pre-RNA transcripts, with a similar function to the conserved RnjA and RnjB, two enzymes found only in gram-positive bacteria.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Transcription  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Transcription is the first step of gene expression, in which a particular segment of DNA is copied into RNA by the enzyme RNA polymerase.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 29 genes involved in transcription, 17 were conserved in all S. aureus strains, including rpoA-D and rpoS, nusABG, greAB, rho and sigBEFH, fliA, and rhiB.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of the 12 genes missing, 7 are also absent from B. subtilis, including rpoE, H, and Z as well as fecL, dmsD, mfd, and torD.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  rpoN was present in both E. coli and B. subtilis but lacking in all S. aureus strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Finally, sigVWXZ were missing in all S. aureus strains, despite being present in B. subtilis, indicating different transcriptional control strategies.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  


Discussion  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The pangenome of S. aureus was assembled and applied to highlight the genetic, metabolic, and pathogenic diversity of the species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A particular emphasis was placed on the analysis of strain-specific metabolic capabilities and the distribution of virulence factors.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Based on the content of the pangenome, GEMs of metabolism were reconstructed and deployed for 64 S. aureus strains to determine their functional differences by computing minimal media compositions and growth capabilities across more than 300 nutrient sources both aerobically and anaerobically.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All strains were predicted to be auxotrophic for niacin and thiamin, whereas strain-specific auxotrophies were predicted for riboflavin, guanine, and leucine among others (Table 1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Model-predicted growth capabilities and identification of virulence factors allowed for the creation of classification schemas that could distinguish groups of strains based on relatively few traits.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The results presented here demonstrate that comparisons of GEM-predicted metabolic capabilities among different strains in a species can be used to identify strain-specific biomarkers and to uncover and elucidate metabolic determinants of virulence.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Identification and characterization of the makeup of a species pangenome is a powerful tool to analyze genomic diversity within a taxon.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The S. aureus pangenome described here is composed of 7,457 unique genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Of these, 1,441 genes are shared among all strains in the species, forming a core genome.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Functional assignment of the core genes revealed that they are mostly associated with housekeeping functions (i.e., control of gene expression machinery and basic biochemistry).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  On average, 56% of genes in the average S. aureus genome are part of the core genome.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This confirms previous observations that S. aureus is a clonal species (22).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Recent studies have shown that even mutations in the core genome of closely related S. aureus isolates can have significant effects on virulence, proliferation, and persistence of S. aureus strains (6).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, a deeper analysis of strain-specific genetic variants in this set could offer further insights into S. aureus pathogenicity.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The 1,441 core genes were aligned to produce a reliable phylogeny compared with those derived from a limited number of genes (Fig S2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In general, the phylogeny is consistent with information from literature including agreement between strains ST and clade.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, some exceptions were observed: (i) the ST5 group is included within a paraphyletic clade corresponding to CC5 and (ii) S. aureus COL (ST250) is placed in the ST8 clade.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Although these exceptions are consistent with the evolutionary history of S. aureus [i.e., the paraphyletic clade includes members of the same clonal complex and ST8 is the predicted ancestor of ST250 (40)], the differences between MLST genotyping and the presented phylogeny underline the limitations of molecular typing techniques to differentiate between closely related strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Furthermore, in regards to host specificity, the distribution of the LA-MRSA strains along the tree indicates that they have originated through different independent events.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In particular, we identified a monophyletic clade of ST398 strains, a clade of strains from different lineages (ST425, ST133, and ST151), and two strains included in different human-associated clades (corresponding to ST5 and ST1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In contrast to the core genome, the size of the pangenome was used to predict, via extrapolation, the number of genome sequences required for bounding the gene repertoire of a clade.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Our regression analysis shows that the S. aureus pangenome is open, indicating that the gene repertoire of this species is theoretically boundless.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This result is in agreement with a previous DNA microarray experiment involving 36 S. aureus strains (41), in which extensive genetic variability was reported.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A high portion of unique genes were found to be related to mobile genetic elements (i.e., transposon, phages, and plasmids), which may drive acquisition of novel functional modules via HGT, including drug resistance and virulence (42).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Beyond these evolutionary insights, the pangenome has important practical implications.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The presence/absence of genes from the dispensable genome (i.e., genes unique to some strains) represents a high-resolution alternative to MLST that we have used to diagnose distinct groups of pathogenic bacteria based on specific biomarkers.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
We specifically examined the presence of virulence factors in the core genomes and pangenomes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Overall, most of the S. aureus virulome is conserved across the 64 strains examined.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  There is only one VF unique to a single strain (i.e., the etb gene, encoding the exfoliative toxin B), whereas 54 VFs were shared by a small number of strains but were not present in all strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Some virulence factors are redundantly present across the species to overcome herd immunity.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, several enterotoxins are found as unique variants in different strains to avoid detection by a dynamic host immune system.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The vast majority of the virulome is composed of core and pseudocore (shared by most of the strains) genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The presence of these genes is interesting from an evolutionary point of view because it implies that S. aureus has evolved a highly conserved system to carry out its infectious cycle.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  From an applied viewpoint, these genes may represent targets for virulence inhibitor or antibody-based therapeutic strategies (43).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To gain insights into the metabolic diversity between S. aureus strains, we produced 64 strain-specific GEMs and used them to simulate growth capabilities in different nutrient sources.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Experimental verification of minimal growth-supporting media was partially confirmed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  It is conceivable that certain infectious niches may activate latent biosynthetic pathways under the appropriate conditions, and the fact that genes in these pathways have not developed into pseudogenes indicates that they may have relevance in niche adaptation (44).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A clustering analysis based on computed metabolic phenotypes distinguished groups of strains from each other.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  One distinction that arose was the identification of USA300 isolate as the only strain with a capability to catabolize spermidine due to the presence of speG encoded spermidine acetlytransferase.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In humans, spermidine is known to potentiate keratinocyte killing of S. aureus in conjunction with antimicrobial peptides (45) and several classes of antibiotics, especially -lactams (46).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Recently, studies have found regions of ACME (including the arginine catabolic arc region) in many different, non-USA300 strains (47, 48).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, the association of the speG locus with ACME is uncommon outside of USA300.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, acquisition of speG has likely been helpful in the rapid spread of USA300 strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The speG gene was horizontally transferred from S. epidermis, a commensal colonizer of human skin (49).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, a future comparison of shared metabolic capabilities between S. aureus strains and S. epidermis might provide further insights.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Furthermore, design of antibiotics that specifically interfere with strain-specific capabilities such as those identified here offer new ways to inhibit epidemiological spread of particularly infectious strains.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The GEMs presented here open the S. aureus species to a wide array of systems biology methods and techniques (50).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, using these models, it is straightforward to predict essential genes in different conditions (we found an average of 190 essential genes for the 64 strains in LB media; Dataset S1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Furthermore, single gene essentiality screens have been extended to the study of synthetic lethality and discovery of synergistic antibiotics (51).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In conclusion, the multiscale comparative approach used in this work provides insights into the diversity of the S. aureus species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Historical methods for classification of S. aureus strains have developed considerably from phage-typing to PFGE and MLST approaches (52).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  New high-throughput DNA sequencing technologies and analysis techniques will continue to improve our ability to track and distinguish pathogens.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Computational analysis of GEMs built for multiple strains in a species provides a powerful tool that can be deployed to provide insights into metabolic determinants of virulence, to identify novel biomarkers capable of distinguishing certain strains from one another, and to discover new pharmacological targets.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Materials_and_Methods  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Tools and methods used to identify and construct the core genome and pangenome of S. aureus as well as the analysis of atypical genes are presented in SI Materials and Methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Briefly, InParanoid (53) was used to identify orthologous genes to construct a pangenome.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The strain-specific model reconstruction procedure was performed with Simpheny (Genomatica, Inc.), and gap-filling algorithms and in silico growth simulation conditions were implemented in Constraints-Based Reconstruction and Analysis Toolbox for Python (COBRApy) (54) and are described in SI Materials and Methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Heat map, phylogenetic tree, and decision tree construction are described in SI Materials and Methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  S. aureus strains NCTC8325, Newman, N315, Mu50, and USA300 were used for carbon source and growth testing.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All experimental protocols are described in SI Materials and Methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

SI_Materials_and_Methods  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Constructing_a_Representative_Dataset_of_S_aureus_Species  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The genomic sequences for all S. aureus strains were downloaded from the ftp site of NCBI (ftp://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genomes/).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To establish the phylogenetic relationship existing between S. aureus representatives, extensive phylogenetic analysis was conducted by using a concatamer of a set of seven housekeeping genes (arcC, aroE, glpF, gmk, pta, tpi, and yqiL) to construct a phylogenetic tree (Fig 1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Multiple sequence alignments were performed with ClustalW (55).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Maximum likelihood (ML) analysis was carried out using Phyml tool (56), with a Whelan and Goldman model of amino acid substitution.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Statistical support at nodes was obtained by nonparametric bootstrapping on 100 resampled datasets.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A set of 64 strains was selected from the dendrogram to create a heterogeneous dataset in terms of (i) drug resistance (MRSA, MSSA, VRSA, and VISA), (ii) host specificity (human vs. animal), (iii) virulence/environmental association (CA-MRSA, HA-MRSA, and LA-MRSA), and (iv) tree topology.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We wanted to sample strains from different clusters of the tree.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Therefore, the final dataset has been designed to be, as far as possible, an unbiased representation of the whole S. aureus species.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Orthologous_Gene_Identification_and_Pangenome_Construction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The homology relationships between genes of each different strain were assessed using InParanoid (57).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Considering that the dataset was composed of 64 S. aureus strains, the systematic use of InParanoid for all of the pairwise combinations resulted in a total of 2,016 InParanoid tables as output, representing the orthologous genes for each genome pair.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These were also used for mapping the genomic contents of all of the S. aureus strains onto the reference GEM of S. aureus N315 (see below).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Representative genes for each cluster were functionally annotated using the COG database (19), allowing us to assign a functional category to each cluster of orthologous genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Estimation_of_Core_Genomes_and_Pangenomes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To compute the pangenome of the S. aureus species, the dataset comprising each S. aureus representative was analyzed using the dgenome module of the Ductape suite [Galardini et al.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  (58)], which allowed for a fast computation of the pangenome using a pairwise best bidirectional hit approach.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The estimation of generalized pangenome metrics, such as core genome and pangenome size, was performed by simulating random pangenomes with number of genomes (N) ranging from 2 to 64.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For each N, a total of 10 random combinations of organisms were sampled.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  From each of these pangenomes, the total number of genes and the number of conserved and new genes were determined, corresponding to pangenome, core, and unique genome sizes, respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Also, these numbers were used to the estimate the pangenome parameters (see below).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As reported by Tettelin et al.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  (7), Heaps law, an empirical law originally used in the field of information retrieval (59), can be used to describe the S. aureus pangenome size and the number of new genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These power laws, N(pan)=k1N and N(new)=k2N, were fitted to the number of total genes and new genes, respectively, to find the parameters giving the best fit, using the curve_fit function of the Python package Scipy.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similarly, to estimate the S. aureus core genome the following double exponential decay function was fit on the number of core genes: N(core)=k1exp(N/1)+k2exp(N/2)+.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To obtain a reliable estimation, the free parameters to be optimized (k1,k2,1,2,and) have been optimized with the curve_fit function with the following initial guesses: 1, 1, 0.1, 0.1, and 1,400, respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Identification_of_Atypical_S_aureus_Genes  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The GC content and codon bias of all core genes was used to detect genes with atypical compositional features.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For each strain, the core GC distribution has been obtained by computing the average GC content of each core gene, which represents genes that have not been transferred; therefore, their GC distribution reflects the GC value of not-transferred genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This has been used to estimate a lower and upper threshold value, corresponding to the values composing 99% of the distribution.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The genes with GC values not included between these values were reported as atypical genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The codon use differences between core genes and pangenes were determined using the Hidden Markov Model (HMM)-based tool (SIGI-HMM) (60, 61) that detects genomic islands based on statistical analysis of codon use with high precision (62).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Strain-Specific_Model_Reconstruction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Genome-scale metabolic reconstructions were available for some S. aureus strains (1214), including a previously published model from our group named iSB619.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This model consists of 615 genes that catalyze 742 reactions.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We first combined this model with other models of S. aureus to form a reference model for S. aureus N315.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Then, we extended this model by adding content from metabolic databases including Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG), SEED (63), and MetaCyc.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Finally, the reconstruction was manually curated to form an updated reconstruction of S. aureus N315 that represents an improved genome-scale representation of the metabolism of this strain, containing 1,475 reactions, 1,232 metabolites, and 850 genes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This model was then used with the genomic sequence of S. aureus N315 as a reference to map the genomic content of other S. aureus strains to obtain a set of shared genes and reactions present in all genomes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All genomes were reannotated using the RAST server (63).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Next, other models of S. aureus strains and related organisms were examined for strain-specific metabolic content.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These included the metabolic models of 13 S. aureus strains (Mu50, MW2, COL, EMRSA-16 strain 252, methicillin-sensitive strain 476, JH1, JH9, RF122, USA300, USA300 TCH1516, Newman, and N315) (14) and the curated model of B. subtilis 168 (iBsu1103) (64).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Additional reaction content was added from ModelSEED (63), KEGG (65, 66), and BIOCYC (67).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All reactions added were manually curated according to published protocol (68).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  MetaNetX (69) was used to standardize metabolites and reactions to Systems Biology Research Group (70) abbreviations.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All genome sequences were downloaded from GenBank (71) on July 16, 2015.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Gene names conform to the NCBI locus name according to the original annotation in GenBank.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Biomass_Composition  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A detailed culture-based biomass composition for S. aureus is not available in literature, so we combined biomass compositions from three previous models of S. aurues (12, 13) to form the biomass composition for the strain-specific models presented here.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These models also assumed that the production rates of metabolites required for cellular growth were similar to those of the related gram-positive organism B. subtilis (64).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We adjusted the fatty acid composition of the biomass function based on S. aureus-specific data (72).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The detailed biomass composition is provided in Dataset S1, worksheet 8 and Table S1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

In_Silico_Growth_Simulations  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Each of the 64 S. aureus models is available as an SBML file (Dataset S1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The models were loaded into the COBRApy toolbox (54) to perform flux balance analysis (FBA) (73).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  M9 minimal media was simulated by setting a lower bound of 1,000 (allowing unlimited uptake) on the exchange reactions for Ca2+, Cl, CO2, Co2+, Cu2+, Fe2+, Fe3+, H+, H2O, K+, Mg2+, Mn2+, MoO42-, Na+, Ni2+, SeO42-, SeO32, and Zn2+.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The default carbon source was glucose with a lower bound of 10, the default nitrogen source was NH4 with a lower bound of 1,000, the default phosphorous source was HPO42 with a default bound of 1,000, and the default sulfur source was SO42 with a default bound of 1,000 (Dataset S1, worksheet 5).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To identify sole growth-supporting carbon, nitrogen, phosphorous, and sulfur sources, each of these default compounds were removed from the media (lower bound set to 0) one at a time, and different compounds were added to determine if they supported growth.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For aerobic simulations, O2 was added with a lower bound of 20 and to 0 for anaerobic simulations.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For models with identified auxotrophies, the compound for which a strain was auxotrophic (Dataset S1, worksheet 6) was also added to the M9 minimal media for each simulation with a lower bound of 1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Model growth phenotypes were determined using FBA one at a time on each condition with the core biomass reaction as the objective.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Nutrient sources with growth rates above zero were classified as growth supporting, whereas nutrient sources with growth rates of zero were classified as non-growth supporting.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The Gurobi 6.0 linear programming solver (Gurobi Optimization, Inc.) was used to perform FBA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Gap_Filling  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The Constraints-Based Reconstruction and Analysis implementation of the SMILEY algorithm (growMatch) (74) was used to predict sets of exchange and gap-filling reactions for models that were unable to simulate biomass in silico on M9 minimal media with glucose aerobically using FBA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The universal set of reactions used to fill gaps was based on MetaNetX.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The Gurobi 5.0.0 mixed-integer linear programming solver was used (Gurobi Optimization Inc.) to implement SMILEY.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  When adding content to enable the strains to grow, exchange reactions indicating strain-specific auxotrophies were prioritized over adding new reactions without genetic evidence.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Glucose (carbon source), phosphate, sulfate, nicotinamide, and thiamine were both experimentally used and computationally verified.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, other substrates, such as the nucleosides cytidine and uridine, were predicted not to be required in their metabolic model.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Prediction_of_All_Growth-Supporting_Carbon__Nitrogen__Phosphorus__and_Sulfur_Sources  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The possible growthsupporting carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur sources of each S. aureus strain were identified using FBA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  First, all exchange reactions for extracellular metabolites containing the four elements were identified from the metabolite formulas.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Every extracellular compound containing carbon was considered a potential carbon source, for example.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Next, to determine possible growthsupporting carbon sources, the lower bound of the glucose exchange reaction was constrained to zero.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Then the lower bound of each carbon exchange reaction was set, one at a time, to 10 mmolgDW1h1 (a typical uptake rate for growthsupporting substrates), and growth was maximized by FBA using the biomass reaction.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The target substrate was considered growth supporting if the predicted growth rate was above zero.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  While identifying carbon sources, the default nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur sources were ammonium (nh4), inorganic phosphate (pi), and inorganic sulfate (so4), respectively.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Prediction of growthsupporting sources of these other three elements was performed in the same manner as growth on carbon, with glucose as the default carbon source.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Prediction_of_Gene_Essentiality  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
To simulate the effects of gene knockouts, each S. aureus model was used with its default constraints and biomass reaction objective.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For growth on glucose, the lower bound of the glucose exchange reaction was set to 10 mmolgDW1h1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  All genes in each model were knocked out one a time, and growth was simulated by FBA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This assessment can be performed by using FBA with the additional constraint of setting the fluxes through all of the reactions that cannot occur without a given gene (i.e., when isozymes independently catalyzing a same reaction are not present) for which the essentiality is being tested equal to zero.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Gene knockout strains with a growth rate above zero were considered nonessential.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Virulome_Construction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A curated set of virulence factors was obtained from the virulence factor database and literature references (30, 75).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The presence of these VFs within the S. aureus genomes was determined using a BLAST search on the curated database using the blastp algorithm with following thresholds: e-value less_than1e-20, sequence similarity 70%, and alignment length 60%.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Heat_Map_and_Phylogenetic_Tree_Construction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
The binary results from the growth/no growth simulations for each strain were used to compute a correlation matrix based on dissimilarity indices calculated using the Jaccard method in the vegdist function of the Vegan R package.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Wards agglomerative clustering of the matrix of correlations was used to cluster the species using the hclust function of the Vegan R package and used to form a dendrogram.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The heat map was visualized using the gplots R package with values aligned based on the calculated dendrogram.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Decision_Tree_Construction  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
A decision tree (Fig 5) was calculated based on growth/no growth values for each strain classified into their major pathotypes: InPec, ExPec, or commensal.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The classification tree tool, part of the Orange Canvas software package (76), was used to calculate and display the decision tree using a Gini Index attribute selection criteria with no binarization, two minimum leaves for prepruning, and m = 2 estimate for postpruning with leaves of the same majority class being recursively merged.  #@NEW_LINE#@#
