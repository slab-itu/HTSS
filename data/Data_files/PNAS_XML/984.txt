article id="http://dx.doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1713137114"  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
title  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Near-infrared remotely triggered drug-release strategies for cancer treatment  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Significance  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Gold-based nanoparticles that absorb near-infrared light have shown the potential to selectively target and treat cancer through highly efficient light-to-heat conversion.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This study shows that gold-based nanoparticles can be coated with drug-bearing host biomolecules for remotely triggerable release.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Near-infrared light-triggered release of docetaxel from a nanoshell-based DNA host complex, and lapatinib from nanoshell-based DNA and human serum albumin host complexes, is demonstrated.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  There is a strong dependence upon the type of near-infrared illuminationcontinuous wave or pulsedspecific to the drug-laden host molecules.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Localizing drug delivery both spatially and temporally by combining nanoshell-based complexes and pulsed-laser irradiation is a promising strategy for highly controlled drug delivery that can apply to a myriad of therapeutic applications.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Abstract  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Remotely controlled, localized drug delivery is highly desirable for potentially minimizing the systemic toxicity induced by the administration of typically hydrophobic chemotherapy drugs by conventional means.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems provide a highly promising approach for localized drug delivery, and are an emerging field of interest in cancer treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Here, we demonstrate near-IR light-triggered release of two drug molecules from both DNA-based and protein-based hosts that have been conjugated to near-infrared-absorbing Au nanoshells (SiO2 core, Au shell), each forming a light-responsive drug delivery complex.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We show that, depending upon the drug molecule, the type of host molecule, and the laser illumination method (continuous wave or pulsed laser), in vitro light-triggered release can be achieved with both types of nanoparticle-based complexes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Two breast cancer drugs, docetaxel and HER2-targeted lapatinib, were delivered to MDA-MB-231 and SKBR3 (overexpressing HER2) breast cancer cells and compared with release in noncancerous RAW 264.7 macrophage cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Continuous wave laser-induced release of docetaxel from a nanoshell-based DNA host complex showed increased cell death, which also coincided with nonspecific cell death from photothermal heating.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Using a femtosecond pulsed laser, lapatinib release from a nanoshell-based human serum albumin protein host complex resulted in increased cancerous cell death while noncancerous control cells were unaffected.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Both methods provide spatially and temporally localized drug-release strategies that can facilitate high local concentrations of chemotherapy drugs deliverable at a specific treatment site over a specific time window, with the potential for greatly minimized side effects.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Results_and_Discussion  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Nanocomplex_Synthesis_and_Characterization  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Schematic representations of nanocomplex fabrication and the light-triggered drug-release process using two host biomolecules bound to Au NSs are shown in Scheme 1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To form the NS@DNA@drug nanocomplexes, dsDNA is first attached to NS through Authiol bonds.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The DNA sequence selected for DTX (DNA1: 5-HS-C6H12-GGA ATA CAC GCG CGA AAT CAC G-3) is a variation of Dickersons dodecamer, d(CGCGAATTCGCG)2, which has previously been shown to complex taxol and other analogs (36).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The base substitutions were made to result in the dsDNA structure having a higher melting point than any hairpin structures, which allows for preferential formation of the DNA duplex upon DNA cooling in the hybridization process.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  An adeninethymine-rich sequence was chosen for LAP (DNA2 = 5-HS-C6H12-AAA AAA ATA TAT AAT TAA AAG TTG AAA-3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  LAPs structure contains a pyrimidinamine, analagous to adenine, and is thus expected to bind to thymine in a similar manner (37).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The NS@DNA@drug nanocomplexes are incubated with cells, then illuminated with a near-IR laser.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The laser wavelength was selected to match the NS plasmon resonance in the near-IR spectral water window (38).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To maximize drug-release efficiency, two illumination sources were investigated: CW laser irradiation and femtosecond pulsed-laser irradiation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  CW illumination results in dehybridization of the DNA and release of the drug, while pulsed irradiation results in AuS bond breakage and release of the dsDNAdrug complex.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
HSA is the most abundant protein in blood plasma and can bind highly insoluble drug molecules in its hydrophobic pockets (3941).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Due to its large size and multiple binding regions, HSA has the potential to significantly increase the payload capacity of the nanocomplexes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  NS are coated with HSA, which forms a protein corona around the particles, which are subsequently incubated with drug molecules (Scheme 1B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The NS@HSA@drug nanocomplexes are subsequently incubated with cells and irradiated with either a CW or pulsed near-IR laser in the same manner as the NS@DNA@drug nanocomplexes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Functionalization of the NS surfaces was verified with transmission electron microscopy (TEM), extinction spectroscopy, zeta potential, and dynamic light scattering (DLS) (Fig 1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The TEM images of unconjugated NS (orange) showed spherical particles with smooth regular surfaces, while TEM images of NS@DNA1 and NS@DNA2 (blue) showed the formation of a thin dsDNA layer around the NS (Fig 1A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  TEM images of NS@DNA1@DTX and NS@DNA2@LAP (green) showed no morphological changes (zoomed-in TEM images of the NS surface are seen in SI Appendix, Fig S1).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the case of NS@HSA and NS@HSA@LAP (Fig 1A), a hard and a soft corona around the NS can be observed in the TEM images of the complexes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The extinction maxima red-shift when coated with dsDNA and further red-shift when the drug molecules are loaded due to the changes in the dielectric environment around the NS (Fig 1B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Protein coating induces a larger red-shift relative to dsDNA as a result of the larger size of the adsorbed protein layer.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  No changes in the peak shape or width of the extinction spectrum is observed, indicating that the nanocomplexes do not aggregate during the functionalization process.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Zeta-potential measurements further confirm modification of the NS surface.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For example, the zeta potential became more negative after dsDNA attachment and even more negative with drug loading (Fig 1B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  DLS measurements show the hydrodynamic diameter of bare NS decreases after DNA functionalization, possibly due to a change in the solvent from water to TE buffer.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The hydrodynamic diameter further decreases after drug loading, likely as a result of compaction of the DNA (42).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the case of protein functionalization, the hydrodynamic diameter increases slightly after protein coating and shows minimal change upon drug loading.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Confirmation_of_Drug_Loading  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) was used to confirm drug loading of the NS@host complexes (Fig 2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The SERS spectrum of the thiolated dsDNA1 monolayer on the NS surface shows several characteristic SERS bands at 1,346 and 1,480 cm1 (43).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The SERS mode at 737 cm1 is weak due to the low number of adenine bases in the DNA sequence (44) (Fig 2A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Moreover, several strong SERS peaks at 1,003, 1,271, 1,520, and 1,543 cm1 assigned to the benzene ring modes (C-C, C-O, C = C, and ring stretching) are seen in the NS@DNA1@DTX SERS spectrum (45, 46).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This indicates the DNA1DTX complex formation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The bands at 1,340 and 1,475 cm1 are enhanced in the complex spectrum in comparison with DNA1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the case of the NS@DNA2 monolayer on the NS surface, the SERS spectrum is dominated by the adenine ring-breathing mode at 737 cm1 (Fig 2B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  LAP Raman peaks including C-O-C mode of a substituted furan at 1,136 cm1 are seen in the NS@DNA2@LAP SERS spectra.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The proteindrug nanocomplex SERS data confirm that LAP has associated with the HSA protein corona on the particle surface from spectral features that include a quinazoline ring stretch at 1,369 cm1 (Fig 2C).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The observed SERS frequencies and their assignments of DNA1, DNA2, and NS@DNA1@DTX, NS@DNA2@LAP complexes are listed in SI Appendix, Table S1.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Additional confirmation of LAP loading onto NS@DNA2 was performed with X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (SI Appendix, Fig S2).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A survey scan shows Au 4p3, 4d3, 4d5, 4f5, and 4f7 from the Au layer of the NS.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Si 2s and 2p peaks, as well as O 1s peaks, originate from the SiO2 core of the NS.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The C 1s peak comes from the DNA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  An elemental scan of the S 2p feature reveals a peak at a binding energy of 168 eV, indicating the presence of S in a sulfone group as opposed to a thiol, which further confirms LAP loading onto the DNA host.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Nanocomplex_Cellular_Uptake  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Cellular uptake of the NS@host@drug complexes was investigated using dark-field, confocal, and reflectance (emission and extinction at 640 nm) microscopy in RAW 264.7 macrophage cells, and in MDA-MB-231-eGFP and SKBR3 breast cancer cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For simplicity, only the RAW 264.7 images are shown (Fig 3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the dark-field images, internalization of the nanocomplexes can easily be seen as time-dependent, diffraction-limited bright spots (Fig 3 B and C).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As a control, cells not incubated with nanoparticles showed no observable bright spots (Fig 3A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Because the dark-field images alone do not give clear evidence whether the NS@HSA@drug are adsorbed onto the outer membrane of the cells or internalized, bright-field images were also obtained.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Varying the depth of field within an individual cell allows for clear 3D visualization of the nanocomplex distribution within the cell.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Fig 3E is a slice from the middle of the cell showing clear diffraction-limited dark spots corresponding to the nanocomplexes, verifying that the NS complexes are internalized within the cell.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As a control, cells not incubated with the nanocomplexes showed no observable dark spots when imaged in the same mode (Fig 3D).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  To further investigate the nanocomplex cellular uptake and localization inside the cells, confocal images were measured in transmission and reflection mode (emission and extinction at 640 nm) at varying depths.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The details in the orthogonal YZ and XZ planes illustrate the location of the nanoparticles within the cell boundary around but not inside the nucleus [the nucleus was stained with Hoechst 33342 dye (Fig 3F)].  #@NEW_LINE#@#  We have previously shown that NS phagocytosed by macrophages are dispersed within vacuoles (34).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  TEM of a macrophage in the necrotic area of a breast tumor shows NS within a vacuole that is likely an endosome/lysosome (SI Appendix, Fig S3).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  

Intracellular_Light-Triggered_Drug-Release_Studies  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Cellular cytotoxicity resulting from light-triggered release of DTX and LAP was evaluated using both the dsDNA and protein host adsorbates.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Cells were grown for 24 h in 96-well plates, incubated for 4 h with nanocomplexes, washed with PBS, then given fresh media.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Cells were irradiated with 1.5 W of CW laser power or a Ti:Sa laser amplifier (Coherent, RegA) operating at 250 kHz with an average power of 25 mW and 160-fs pulsed-laser power for 2 min, then incubated at 37 Â°C, 5% CO2 for 24 h to allow time for the released drug molecules to interact with the cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Cell death was evaluated with a lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) cytotoxicity assay.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Percent cytotoxicity values were calculated considering 10% cell death in the control experiment, NS@host with no laser treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
DTX release from the DNA1 host was investigated in MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells (Fig 4A) and in RAW 264.7 macrophage cells (Fig 4B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The NS@DNA1@DTX nanocomplex is shown to be nontoxic, since statistically no increase in cytotoxicity was observed compared with the NS@DNA1 nanoparticles.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This indicates that DTX stays confined within the DNA host and that no leaching occurs.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A very statistically significant (P less_than 0.01) increase in cytotoxicity for the cells with NS@DNA1 after CW laser treatment (no DTX) is likely due to photothermal ablation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A statistically significant (P less_than 0.05) increase in cell death is observed under CW laser irradiation between no DTX and DTX, confirming there is a positive effect of drug release beyond any photothermal heating caused by the laser.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  A very statistically significant (P less_than 0.01) increase in cell death is seen when DTX is released with the CW laser compared with NS@DNA1@DTX with no laser, while no increased cell death is seen for pulsed-laser-induced release.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This is most likely due to the different DNA release mechanisms resulting from CW vs. pulsed lasers (30).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In the case of a CW laser, the nanocomplex and its surrounding local environment undergo a photothermal temperature increase, causing the dsDNA to dehybridize, releasing the drug and ssDNA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Similar results are seen for both the cancer and macrophage cells, which are to be expected, since DTX is not a targeted cancer drug and does not discriminate between cancerous and healthy cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  There is an extremely statistically significant (P less_than 0.001) increase in cytotoxicity for NS@DNA1@DTX without and with CW laser.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  In contrast, irradiation with a pulsed laser induces breakage of the AuS anchoring bond of the dsDNA.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As a result, the entire dsDNAdrug complex is released, with the drug likely remaining sequestered within the DNA: this should restrict DTX from inducing cell death upon irradiation.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
In principle, pulsed-laser-induced drug release is preferable over CW laser-induced drug release, since far lower average powers can be used, minimizing photothermal heating effects that would include nonspecific cell death.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Since pulsed-laser-induced drug release from the NS@DNA host releases the entire dsDNAdrug complex, NS@HSA was investigated as an alternative drug host.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  LAP was released from both an NS@DNA and NS@HSA host in SKBR3 breast cancer cells and RAW 264.7 macrophage cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  LAP was chosen to see if cell death could be preferentially induced in cancer cells over healthy cells, since LAP specifically targets the HER2 receptor that is overexpressed on SKBR3 cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  For the NS@DNA host, no statistically significant increase in cytotoxicity is observed for either CW or pulsed-laser-induced release for SKBR3 cells (Fig 5A).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  There is also no increase in cytotoxicity for CW laser-induced release from the protein scaffold; however, there is a very statistically significant (P less_than 0.01) increase in cytotoxicity for pulsed-laser-induced LAP release from the NS@HSA host in SKBR3 cells (Fig 5B).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The low average power (25 mW) does not increase the local temperature and therefore cells with NS@HSA alone do not show increased death after pulsed-laser treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  However, pulsed-laser irradiation is clearly sufficient to alter the HSA, releasing LAP from the NS@HSA@LAP nanocomplex, shown by the extremely statistically significant (P less_than 0.001) increase in cytotoxicity vs. pulsed irradiation of NS@HSA without LAP.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The extent to which the NS-bound HSA is unfolded or denatured by femtosecond laser irradiation is unclear, and may be the subject of future studies.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
LAP release was performed in the noncancerous macrophage cells as a control (Fig 5C).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Since macrophages lack the HER2 receptor, LAP release is not expected to induce cytotoxicity.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  There is a very statistically significant (P less_than 0.01) increase in cell death for NS@HSA with laser vs. no laser.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This is likely due to photothermal heating.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  No selective increase in cytotoxicity for CW or pulsed-laser LAP release from the NS@HSA host was observed.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The lack of increased cytotoxicity after LAP release from the NS@HSA host with a pulsed laser confirms that the laser treatment did not directly induce the cell death seen with the LAP release from the NS@HSA host for SKBR3 cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This suggests that the released LAP can selectively induce cell death in HER2 expressing breast cancer cells, without affecting noncancerous cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  


Conclusion  #@NEW_LINE#@#  
Both CW and pulsed near-IR light have been shown to induce in vitro release of drug molecules from NS@DNA and NS@HSA complexes.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  DTX and targeted LAP, two commonly used breast cancer drugs, were released in both cancerous and noncancerous cells.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The results demonstrate that there is a higher percent cytotoxicity for CW vs. pulsed-laser-induced DTX release from a DNA host; however, some nonspecific cell death is induced by the CW laser itself.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Using the protein scaffold, increased cytotoxicity was observed for LAP release using pulsed vs. CW light in cancerous cells, while noncancerous cells were unaffected.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  These results show that light-triggered drug release could be a very promising method for delivering chemotherapy drugs in cancer treatment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  By simply exchanging the chemotherapy drug, this system can be extended to treatments for many cancer types.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The flexible loading and release strategies shown here will allow release to be more easily controlled and tailored for various chemotherapeutic drugs and cancer types.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  This in turn has the potential for increasing therapeutic efficacy by delivering high local drug concentrations while keeping systemic drug concentrations low, which would reduce the well-known deleterious side effects of conventional chemotherapeutic drug delivery methods.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The hosting of LAP within HSA deserves specific comment.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Tyrosine kinase inhibitors are notoriously insoluble in water, which is one of the reasons why this class of drug has poor oral bioavailability (47).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  Additionally, although clinical trials have demonstrated that the combination of LAP with other HER2 directed therapies for dual blockade is of benefit in certain subgroups of patients (48), severe toxicities, diarrhea in particular, are leading to poor compliance and treatment discontinuation (49).  #@NEW_LINE#@#  The NS@HSA@LAP formulation has the potential to deliver an efficacious dose of a poorly soluble drug directly to the tumor where it can be released.  #@NEW_LINE#@#  As it will be released only at the tumor, theoretically the systemic dose will be less than with current oral formulations resulting in less toxicity, more compliance, and greater disease control.  #@NEW_LINE#@#
